# SHAMASH AND THE CIRCLE: How Divine Justice Radiates Through Geometry
## Grade 3 | 100-Minute Speech with 2 Embedded Demonstrations

**Speech Title:** *Shamash and the Circle: How Divine Justice Radiates Through Geometry*  
**Myth:** Shamash (Sun God)  
**Geometric Element:** The Circle  
**Metaphor:** Divine Justice, Truth, and Light Radiating Outward  

---

## PART ONE: THE RITUAL & MATERIAL WORLD (0-50 MINUTES)

### OPENING: THE EYE THAT SEES ALL (0-3 min)

Good morning. I want you to imagine something with me. Close your eyes for just a moment. 

Picture this: You are standing in ancient Mesopotamia, in the city of Ur, over four thousand years ago. It's early morning. The world is still dark and quiet. And then... slowly... the sun rises. Not just any sunrise. In this moment, the people around you begin to whisper prayers. They kneel. They bow.

Why?

Because they believe that the sun is not just a ball of fire in the sky. The sun is **Shamash**—the god of justice, the god of truth, the god who *sees everything*.

Open your eyes.

Shamash can see you when you tell the truth. Shamash can see you when you lie. Shamash sees into your heart. And Shamash does not tolerate injustice.

The ancient Mesopotamians had a saying: "Shamash brings light to hidden things." Everything hidden becomes visible when Shamash looks at it. And Shamash looks at *everything*, because light travels in all directions.

Today, we're going to follow Shamash's light—literally. We're going to trace how the sun's light, which radiates outward in all directions equally, became a mathematical shape: the **circle**. And we're going to see how this simple shape—a circle—became the symbol of divine justice, truth, and perfect order.

And most importantly, we're going to learn to *see* circles everywhere around us, just like Shamash sees everything.

**[IMAGE 1: Shamash tablet - the sun god standing, with rays of light emanating outward in all directions. The rays should be clearly visible, creating a circular pattern.]**

---

### SECTION 1: SHAMASH THE SUN GOD (3-12 min)

In Mesopotamian mythology, Shamash is one of the most powerful gods. He is the god of the sun, but more importantly, he is the god of **justice**, **law**, and **truth**.

Let me tell you about Shamash:

Every single morning, Shamash climbs into his chariot—pulled by powerful horses—and rides across the sky. He starts in the east, where the sun rises. He travels all the way across the sky in an arc—a *circular arc*—and he ends up in the west, where the sun sets.

But here's the most important part: As Shamash travels across the sky, he **sees everything**. He sees into every house. He sees into every heart. He sees justice and injustice. He sees truth and lies. He sees kindness and cruelty.

And Shamash is *angry* at injustice. He punishes those who cheat. He punishes those who steal. He protects those who are honest and fair.

In ancient Mesopotamia, if you were a judge—someone who decided what was fair in court—you would whisper a prayer to Shamash before you made your decision. You would say: "Shamash, you see all. Help me see the truth. Help me be just."

**[IMAGE 2: Shamash's chariot racing across the sky. Show the arc of the sun's path from east to west. The image should emphasize the circular motion.]**

If you were a merchant—someone who bought and sold goods in the market—you would invoke Shamash's name when you made a deal. You would say: "Shamash sees this agreement. Let both of us be honest." Because you knew that Shamash was watching.

If you were someone who had been wronged, you could appeal to Shamash for justice. You could say: "Shamash, this person cheated me. Shamash sees the truth. Shamash, bring justice."

And the people of Mesopotamia believed that Shamash *would* bring justice. Not always immediately. But eventually. Because Shamash never stops watching.

**[IMAGE 3: The symbol of Shamash - a perfect circle with rays extending outward like spokes on a wheel. The sun disk should be very clear, showing perfect geometric symmetry.]**

---

### SECTION 2: THE SYMBOL OF SHAMASH - THE SOLAR DISC (12-25 min)

Now, how did the Mesopotamians represent Shamash visually? How did they draw the sun god?

They drew a **circle**.

But not just any circle. They drew a circle with **rays** extending outward in all directions. These rays represented Shamash's light, spreading outward equally in every direction. The rays represented justice, spreading equally to all people. The rays represented truth, illuminating everything.

This symbol appears *everywhere* in Mesopotamian art and architecture. Let me show you where:

**On Boundary Stones (Kudurrus)**

One of the most important places Shamash's circle appears is on **boundary stones**. These are large pieces of stone that marked the borders of a person's land. They were like property markers—like the fence posts we use today to show "this land is mine, and that land is yours."

But these boundary stones were not just practical markers. They were sacred objects. They were covered with the symbols of gods. And Shamash's solar disc **always appeared at the very top**.

Why? Because Shamash was the witness to the boundary. Shamash was saying: "I see this boundary. I see it clearly. If anyone moves this stone or steals this land, *I will know*."

**[IMAGE 4: A Kudurru (boundary stone) photographed from the front. Shamash's solar disc should be clearly visible at the top of the stone, with other divine symbols below. The disc should show rays radiating outward.]**

The solar disc on the boundary stone meant: "This boundary is sacred. This boundary is watched by Shamash. Do not cheat. Do not steal."

And the Mesopotamians took this very seriously. A boundary stone was sacred. Moving it meant inviting Shamash's punishment.

**On Temples and Public Buildings**

Shamash's symbol also appears on temples—the sacred buildings where people went to worship the gods. The solar disc was carved or painted on doorways, on walls, on roofs.

Why? Because temples were places of justice. In temples, people settled disputes. In temples, people made important agreements. In temples, people swore oaths. And Shamash needed to be present as a witness to all of it.

**[IMAGE 5: A temple wall or entrance showing the solar disc carved or painted prominently. The disc should be the focal point, positioned above a doorway or at the top of the wall.]**

**On Personal Objects - Seals and Amulets**

But Shamash's circle didn't only appear on big, important places. It also appeared on personal objects—things that ordinary people carried with them.

People wore **amulets**—small protective charms—that had Shamash's solar disc engraved on them. When you wore Shamash's circle, you were saying: "Shamash is with me. Shamash sees me. Shamash will help me be just and truthful."

**[IMAGE 6: Several Mesopotamian amulets showing solar disc designs. The images should show different interpretations of the sun disc - some with rays, some more geometric, all circular. Include close-ups.]**

---

### SECTION 3: THE GEOMETRY OF SHAMASH'S LIGHT (25-38 min)

Now here's where mathematics comes in.

When Shamash's light radiates outward from the sun, it travels in *straight lines*. But light travels in *every* direction from the sun simultaneously. 

Imagine you are standing at a certain distance from a candle. The candle's light reaches you. But it also reaches someone standing to your left, and someone to your right, and someone above you, and someone below you. The light is reaching everyone at the same distance from the candle *equally*.

If you measure the distance from the center of the candle to where the light reaches you, and then you measure the distance from the center of the candle to where the light reaches your friend standing next to you, you get the *same distance*.

And if everyone stands at the exact same distance from the candle—if everyone stands on an *invisible circle* around the candle—then everyone receives the light equally.

**This is the mathematical definition of a circle:**

A circle is a **collection of all points that are the exact same distance from a center point**.

**[IMAGE 7: Diagram showing a circle with multiple radius lines drawn from the center to the edge. All the radius lines should be the same length. A candle or sun should be at the center, with light rays extending outward.]**

Let me make this concrete for you. Imagine I'm the sun. Imagine all of you are standing around me—some very close, some farther away.

**SETUP FOR DEMONSTRATION 1 (38 min):**

I'm going to do something. I'm going to mark all the places where you would be if you stood at *exactly* the same distance from me. We call this distance the **radius**.

Everyone who stands at exactly 10 feet from me, raise your hand.

*[Pause. Acknowledge students who understand the concept.]*

If you all stood in a circle around me, all at 10 feet away, where would you be? You'd form a shape—a **circle** with me at the center and you on the edge.

---

### DEMONSTRATION 1: THE RADIANT CIRCLE (38-48 min)

**[LIVE DEMONSTRATION - This is a fully scripted activity]**

**What You'll Need:**
- A ball of string (or rope)
- Scissors
- A central point (could be you standing still, or a chair, or a drawn dot on the ground)
- An open space (hallway, gymnasium, or cleared classroom area)
- Optional: tape or chalk to mark the circle

**Setup (2 minutes):**

1. Tie one end of the string to a fixed point (or hold it yourself at a central location)
2. Measure out a piece of string that is exactly 10 feet long (or 6 feet—whatever works in your space)
3. Cut the string at that length
4. This piece of string represents the **radius**—the distance from the center (Shamash) to the edge (where people stand)

**The Activity (8 minutes):**

"Now, everyone is going to help me draw a circle. You are going to become the circle. 

I'm going to hold one end of this string. You are each going to hold a part of the string and walk around me, keeping the string tight. Keep it at exactly this length—the radius. Don't pull it tight so it stretches, and don't let it go slack.

As you walk in a circle around me, staying at exactly this distance, you will trace out a perfect circle. Ready?"

*[Have students walk slowly around the center point, keeping the string tight and at the same length. If the string is too short for all students, do this in multiple rounds, or use a longer string.]*

**What Students Will Observe:**
- Everyone walks in a curved path, not a straight path
- Everyone stays at exactly the same distance from the center
- The path they walk makes a perfect circle
- All points on the circle are equally far from the center

**The Key Insight (talking as they walk, or after they're done):**

"Do you see what's happening? Each of you is at a different location, but you are all at the *exact same distance* from the center. You are all on a circle.

This is Shamash's justice. Shamash's light reaches everyone equally, no matter where you stand, as long as you are at the same distance from the sun.

The circle is the shape of **equal reach**. The circle is the shape of **fairness**. Everyone on the circle is treated the same by the center.

This is why Shamash's symbol is a circle. Because Shamash's justice is fair to everyone equally."

**[IMAGE 8: Photograph of students walking in a circle around a central point, holding a string taut. The overhead view would show the perfect circular path they're creating.]**

**Conclusion of Demonstration 1 (1 minute):**

"You have just drawn Shamash's circle with your bodies. You were the rays of light, spreading equally in all directions from the center."

### SECTION 4: SHAMASH'S CIRCLE IN DAILY LIFE - MATERIAL OBJECTS (48-50 min)

So far, we've talked about Shamash's symbol on boundary stones and temples. But where did ordinary Mesopotamians see Shamash's circle in their daily lives?

Everywhere.

**On Pottery and Vessels**

Potters would paint or carve Shamash's solar disc on water jugs, grain jars, oil containers—all the practical objects people used every day. Why? Because these objects held things of value. Shamash was protecting them. Shamash was saying: "What is inside this jar is honest and fair. It has not been cheated."

**[IMAGE 9: Mesopotamian pottery with solar disc designs. Show jars and vessels with circular sun symbols painted or carved on them.]**

**On Jewelry and Personal Adornment**

Women and men would wear circular amulets or brooches—often showing Shamash's sun disc—as personal protection. Not just protection from danger, but protection from *guilt*. Wearing Shamash's circle meant you were committed to honesty and justice.

**[IMAGE 10: Mesopotamian jewelry featuring circular designs and solar motifs. Show amulets, brooches, and beads.]**

**In City Planning**

Even the layout of cities reflected Shamash's circle. Many Mesopotamian cities had circular or semi-circular defensive walls. Why? Because a circle is the most efficient shape for defending a city. The circle encompasses the most area with the least wall length. And the circle reminds everyone: "We are under the eye of Shamash. Shamash sees everything that happens in this city."

**[IMAGE 11: Aerial or reconstructed view of a Mesopotamian city with circular or semi-circular walls. The circular layout should be clear.]**

## BRIDGE: FROM RITUAL TO FUNCTION (50-60 min)

Now we transition from the ritual and artistic meaning of the circle to its practical, *functional* meaning. And the perfect bridge is a real Mesopotamian technology: the **potter's wheel**.

The potter's wheel is where the circle moves from being a symbol of Shamash's justice... to being a tool that *solves problems*.

A potter needed to create vessels that were perfectly round, perfectly symmetrical, and strong enough to hold grain, water, or oil. Before the potter's wheel was invented, this was extremely difficult. You would coil clay like rope and build up walls, and it would come out lumpy and uneven.

But with the potter's wheel—a rotating circle—you could create perfect circular vessels. The clay, spinning at the center, naturally forms a circle. The potter's hands guide it, but the rotation does the work.

**So the circle transforms:**
- From a symbol (Shamash's justice)
- Into a technology (potter's wheel)
- Into an economic system (potters creating goods for trade)
- Into mathematics (understanding why circles work)

**[IMAGE 12: Ancient potter's wheel in use, with clay being shaped. The rotational motion and the circular shape of both the wheel and the emerging pot should be clear.]**

## PART TWO: THE MATHEMATICS & MECHANICS (60-100 minutes)

### OPENING THE SECOND HALF: WHY CIRCLES ARE SPECIAL (60-65 min)

We've seen circles everywhere in Mesopotamian culture. We've drawn them. We've walked in them. But now we ask: **Why is the circle so special mathematically?**

Why not use squares? Why not use triangles? Why do circles show up again and again in Mesopotamian innovation and design?

The answer: **Because circles have unique mathematical properties that no other shape has.**

Let me show you.

---

### SECTION 5: THE UNIQUE PROPERTIES OF CIRCLES (65-78 min)

**Property 1: All Points Equidistant from Center**

We already know this one from our demonstration. Every point on a circle is exactly the same distance from the center. This is *unique* to circles. In a square, the corners are not all the same distance from the center—the corners are farther than the midpoints of the sides.

**[IMAGE 13: Diagram showing a circle with multiple radius lines (all the same length) and a square with diagonal lines (showing that corners are farther from center than side midpoints).]**

**Property 2: The Circle is the Most Efficient Shape**

Here's a mind-blowing fact: If you want to enclose the *maximum area* with a *fixed perimeter* (a fixed length of boundary), the shape you use must be a **circle**.

In other words, if I give you a rope of a certain length, and I tell you to arrange it into a shape that encloses as much area as possible, what shape should you make? A circle. Not a square. Not a rectangle. A circle.

Why does this matter for ancient Mesopotamia? Because:
- If you're building a wall around a city, the circle lets you protect the most people with the least amount of wall material
- If you're making a pot, the circular shape holds more grain with less clay
- If you're digging a well, a circular well is stronger and holds more water

**[IMAGE 14: Diagram showing three shapes with the same perimeter (a circle, a square, and a rectangle). The circle should be clearly shown to enclose more area than the others.]**

**Property 3: Radial Symmetry - Perfect Balance**

A circle has **infinite lines of symmetry**. If you draw any line through the center, it divides the circle into two perfectly equal halves. Rotate the circle by any amount, and it looks the same.

This mathematical perfection is why circles represented justice and divine order. A circle is perfectly balanced. There's no "more important side." No side is favored over another. It's fair to everyone equally.

**[IMAGE 15: Circle diagram showing multiple lines of symmetry radiating from the center. Each line divides the circle into two equal halves.]**

**Property 4: The Circumference-Diameter Relationship**

Here's where it gets really interesting. If you measure around the edge of a circle—that's called the **circumference**—and then divide it by the width of the circle—that's called the **diameter**—you always get the same answer.

The answer is approximately **3**.

**[Actual relationships: Circumference ÷ Diameter ≈ 3.14, which we call π (pi)]**

The Mesopotamians discovered this! They didn't call it "pi." They used the approximation of 3. But they discovered that this ratio is *constant*—it's the same for every circle, no matter how big or small.

This was revolutionary. This meant the universe followed mathematical laws. This meant you could *predict* things about circles by using this ratio.

**[IMAGE 16: Circle with diameter marked as a line through the center. Circumference shown as a line unwrapped around the edge. The ratio is illustrated: Circumference ÷ Diameter ≈ 3.]**

### DEMONSTRATION 2: DISCOVERING THE CIRCUMFERENCE-DIAMETER RATIO (78-90 min)

**[HANDS-ON ACTIVITY - Measuring Circular Objects]**

**What You'll Need:**
- Several circular objects: plates, bowls, cans, jars, coins, bottle caps, circular containers of different sizes
- Measuring tape or string
- Ruler or measuring stick
- Paper and pencil
- Calculator (optional, for older students)

**Setup (2 minutes):**

Display the circular objects. Explain: "We are going to discover the same mathematical relationship that the ancient Mesopotamians discovered. We're going to measure circles and find a pattern."

**The Activity (10 minutes):**

1. **Measure the Diameter:** For each circular object, measure across the widest part through the center. This is the *diameter*. Write it down.

2. **Measure the Circumference:** Wrap the string or measuring tape around the outside of the circle. Then measure the string. This is the *circumference*. Write it down.

3. **Calculate the Ratio:** Divide the circumference by the diameter.
   - Example: If circumference = 9 inches and diameter = 3 inches, then 9 ÷ 3 = 3

**What Students Will Discover:**

When they calculate circumference ÷ diameter for different circular objects, they'll get answers around **3**. Some will be exactly 3. Some will be a little more, like 3.1 or 3.2. But they'll all be *close to 3*.

The point: **The ratio is always approximately the same, no matter the size of the circle.**

**Discussing the Discovery (2 minutes):**

"Do you see what happened? We measured circles of different sizes. Some were tiny circles. Some were huge circles. Some were just slightly bigger than others. But in every single case, when we divided the circumference by the diameter, we got an answer close to 3.

This is a **universal law**. It works for every circle in the world. It worked for circles 4,000 years ago in Mesopotamia. It works for circles today.

The ancient Mesopotamians discovered this! They realized that even though circles look different—some big, some small—they all follow this same rule. This is *mathematics*. This is finding patterns in nature that work every time."

**The Mathematical Implication:**

"This means if you know the diameter of a circle, you can calculate how far around it is without measuring. Just multiply the diameter by 3 (or about 3.14 for more accuracy).

For the ancient Mesopotamians, this was incredibly useful:
- City planners could calculate how much wall material they needed
- Potters could plan their clay
- Merchants could calculate container capacity
- Astronomers could understand planetary circles"

**[IMAGE 17: Photograph of students measuring different circular objects with string and rulers. Multiple measurements visible on a chart, showing the discovered ratio.]**

**[IMAGE 18: Chart or whiteboard showing the measurements and ratios for each circular object. All calculations should show results near 3, demonstrating the universal principle.]**

### SECTION 6: CIRCLES IN MESOPOTAMIAN INNOVATION (90-100 min)

So the circle—this simple shape—had both **spiritual meaning** (Shamash's justice) and **mathematical properties** (efficiency, symmetry, universal ratios). But here's the key: the mathematical properties *enabled innovations*.

**Innovation 1: The Potter's Wheel**

The potter's wheel is circular. The potter stands at the center. As the wheel rotates, the clay naturally wants to form a circle. Once potters understood the circle's properties—that all points on a circle are equidistant from center, that circles are efficient, that circles are structurally sound—they could create vessels that were perfectly proportioned, strong, and beautiful.

**[IMAGE 19: Ancient potter's wheel (museum artifact or reconstruction). Show the circular design and how it functions.]**

**Innovation 2: The Chariot Wheel**

Eventually, the Mesopotamians realized that a wheel mounted on an axle could *roll*. A circular wheel, spinning on a fixed axle, could move heavy objects. This led to chariots for transportation, for warfare, for commerce.

**[IMAGE 20: Ancient Mesopotamian chariot with detailed wheels visible. Show the spokes, the hub, the axle clearly.]**

**Innovation 3: Circular Defensive Walls**

City planners realized that circular walls were more efficient than square walls (they enclosed more area) and more effective for defense (there were no corners for enemies to hide in). The circle was the optimal shape for a city wall.

**[IMAGE 21: Aerial or reconstructed view of a circular or semi-circular city wall, showing how it efficiently encloses the city.]**

**Innovation 4: The Cylinder Seal**

Mesopotamians invented cylinder seals—small rolling cylinders that, when rolled across clay, created a repeating pattern. These seals had circular symmetry. They could be rolled repeatedly, creating endless impressions. This was the world's first **mass production technology**.

And why did cylinders work better than any other shape? Because they're circular. They roll smoothly. They repeat perfectly.

**[IMAGE 22: Close-up of cylinder seals from different eras, and a photograph or diagram showing how they roll across clay to create patterns.]**

**The Synthesis:**

From ritual symbol (Shamash's justice) → to practical tool (potter's wheel) → to transportation (chariots) → to defense (city walls) → to communication (cylinder seals).

All of it flows from **understanding circles**.

**[IMAGE 23: Timeline or visual showing the progression from Shamash symbol → to pottery → to wheels → to chariots → to city planning. Show how each innovation builds on the mathematical principles of circles.]**

### CLOSING: THE ENDURING CIRCLE (100 minutes)

We're out of time, but I want to leave you with this thought:

The circle appears in Mesopotamian art and technology thousands of years ago. And it appears in *our* modern world, too. Right now, as I speak:

- **Wheels** are still spinning on cars, bicycles, trains
- **Pottery** is still being made on potters' wheels
- **Cities** are still designed with round buildings and circular parks
- **Clocks** are still circular, measuring time
- **Planets** still orbit in circles (well, nearly circles)
- **Light** still radiates outward in all directions from a source

The ancient Mesopotamians looked at the sun and said: "There is a god in that circle. That god is just. That god is truth. That god is watching us."

And they were right—not about a literal god in the sun, but about the *principle*. The circle is special. The circle is fair. The circle is the shape of cosmic order.

Next week, we'll explore a new shape: the **star**. The star, which represents a *different* god, a *different* kind of power.

But carry this with you: When you see a circle anywhere—on a coin, on a plate, on a building, on a person's face—remember Shamash. Remember that the circle is the shape of justice, the shape of fairness, the shape of universal law.
