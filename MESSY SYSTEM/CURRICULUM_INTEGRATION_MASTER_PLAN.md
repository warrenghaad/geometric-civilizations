#### Plimpton 322: Pythagoras Before Pythagoras!

**From your guide:**
> **Plimpton 322: The Pythagorean Theorem (Before Pythagoras!)**
>
> Another famous tablet is Plimpton 322, dating to around 1900-1600 BCE. This small clay tablet (5 inches wide, 3.5 inches tall) contains a table of numbers that show Pythagorean triples—special number combinations for right triangles.
>
> **What's mind-blowing:**
> This tablet was created at least 1,000 years BEFORE Pythagoras was even born! The Mesopotamians understood right triangle relationships long before the Greeks.
>
> **The 3-4-5 Triangle:**
> The most famous Pythagorean triple is 3-4-5:
> - 3² + 4² = 5²
> - 9 + 16 = 25
> - It works!
>
> **How Mesopotamians used it:**
> - Building foundations (creating square corners for temples)
> - Field measurement (dividing land into rectangular plots)
> - Canal construction (ensuring ditches ran perpendicular to rivers)
> - Ziggurat construction (laying out right-angle corners)
>
> **Where it is now:** Columbia University's Rare Book & Manuscript Library
>
> **Activity:** Make a rope with 12 equal segments (use string with knots). Form a triangle with sides 3-4-5. Measure the corner angle—it's exactly 90°! This is how ancient builders made perfect right angles.

### AFTER (Specific - Using Your Resources):

> **Section 3: Ancient Art and Artifacts**
>
> **Artifact #1: The Adda Seal - Shamash Rising Between Mountains**
>
> In 1866, the British Museum acquired one of the most famous cylinder seals ever found. Today it's known as **The Adda Seal (Museum Number 89115)**.
>
> **When it was made:** 2300 BCE (over 4,300 years ago!)
> **What it's made of:** Greenstone (a beautiful green stone)
> **How big it is:** 3.7 cm tall, 2.55 cm in diameter (about the size of your thumb)
> **Where it is now:** British Museum, London
>
> **What you see on this amazing seal:**
>
> The center shows **Shamash, the sun god**, with rays of light shooting out from his shoulders like sunshine. He's rising up between two mountains with flat, square tops. In his hand, he holds a serrated blade—this blade represents justice cutting through lies!
>
> Next to Shamash stands **Ishtar**, the goddess of love and war. She's shown facing forward (very rare in Mesopotamian art!) with wings spread wide and weapons sticking out of her back.
>
> On the other side is **Ea** (also called Enki), the god of water and wisdom. Streams of water flow from his shoulders, showing his power over rivers and oceans.
>
> Below all the gods, a lion paces along, representing royal power.
>
> At the top of the seal, you can see cuneiform writing—a two-line inscription identifying the seal's owner!
>
> **The Circle Geometry Hidden in This Seal:**
>
> 1. **The seal itself is a cylinder** - when you look at it from the end, it's a perfect circle!
>
> 2. **Shamash's rays spread out in a radial pattern** - Count the rays. They're spaced at equal angles around him. If there are 8 rays, each one is 45° apart (because 360° ÷ 8 = 45°).
>
> 3. **When rolled on wet clay, it creates a circular pattern** - The images repeat in a continuous loop as the cylinder rotates.
>
> **Activity: Become an Ancient Detective!**
>
> 1. Go to britishmuseum.org/collection
> 2. Search for "89115" or "Adda Seal"
> 3. Look at the high-resolution photos
> 4. Count Shamash's rays - how many are there?
> 5. Use a protractor on a printout to measure the angles between rays
> 6. Does it match the formula 360° ÷ number of rays?
>
> **Why This Seal Matters:**
>
> The person who owned this seal 4,300 years ago would roll it across wet clay to "sign" important documents—like contracts about land, business deals, or temple offerings. Every time they rolled it, they were making circles! The seal was their personal signature, and no one else had one exactly like it.
>
> When archaeologists found this seal, it helped them understand that Mesopotamians knew about:
> - Circular geometry
> - Equal division of circles
> - Radial symmetry
> - Cylinder shapes
>
> All these mathematical concepts are hidden in a tiny piece of greenstone carved over 4,000 years ago!
>
