# CRITICAL DESIGN PRINCIPLES
## Assessment Question Hierarchy & Visual Requirements for All Materials

---

## ASSESSMENT QUESTION HIERARCHY - MANDATORY

Every assessment component (oral questions, worksheet prompts, discussion questions, reflection prompts) MUST follow this hierarchy:

### THE PROGRESSION:

**1. WHAT Questions** (Bottom of the Barrel - Basic Level)
- Identification
- Recognition  
- Naming
- Listing
- Observing

**Examples:**
- "What shapes do you see?"
- "What pattern repeats here?"
- "What is this geometric element called?"
- "What deity is shown in this image?"

**Purpose:** Establishes baseline - do they see/recognize the elements?

---

**2. HOW Questions** (Process Level)
- Describing procedures
- Explaining steps
- Demonstrating process
- Showing construction

**Examples:**
- "How is this pattern created?"
- "How many times does the shape rotate?"
- "How do you construct a 6-pointed rosette?"
- "How did the Mesopotamians create this symmetry?"

**Purpose:** Shows procedural understanding - can they describe/replicate the process?

---

**3. WHY / WHY NOT Questions** 💰 **MONEY QUESTIONS** (Reasoning Level)
- Justification
- Explanation of reasoning
- Cause and effect
- Purpose and meaning
- Alternatives evaluated

**Examples:**
- "Why did Mesopotamians use 8-pointed stars for Ishtar?"
- "Why does rotational symmetry create a sense of balance?"
- "Why not use 5 points instead of 8?"
- "Why would this geometric pattern represent divine order?"
- "Why does this pattern work mathematically?"
- "Why wouldn't a random arrangement serve the same purpose?"

**Purpose:** THIS IS WHERE REAL LEARNING IS ASSESSED. Can they reason about geometric choices? Do they understand cultural significance? Can they justify mathematical thinking?

---

**4. WHAT IF Questions** 💰 **MONEY QUESTIONS** (Transfer & Creative Level)
- Hypothetical scenarios
- Transfer to new contexts
- Creative application
- Predicting outcomes
- Connecting to other domains

**Examples:**
- "What if we changed this to 5-fold symmetry instead of 4-fold?"
- "What if the Sumerians had wanted to represent chaos instead of order - what geometric choices might they make?"
- "What if you were designing a modern building using these Mesopotamian principles?"
- "What if we removed one line of symmetry - how would the meaning change?"
- "What if you applied this pattern to music or dance?"

**Purpose:** THIS IS THE HIGHEST LEVEL. Can they transfer understanding? Think creatively within constraints? Apply geometric reasoning to novel situations?

---

## WHY "WHY/WHY NOT" AND "WHAT IF" ARE THE MONEY QUESTIONS:

**WHAT questions** → Anyone can memorize answers
**HOW questions** → Can follow procedures without understanding

**WHY/WHY NOT questions** → Forces justification and reasoning. Students must:
- Explain their thinking
- Connect multiple concepts
- Understand relationships
- Evaluate alternatives
- Demonstrate deep comprehension

**WHAT IF questions** → Reveals transfer and creativity. Students must:
- Apply learning to new contexts
- Think hypothetically
- Reason about variations
- Demonstrate flexible understanding
- Create novel applications

**These money questions reveal:**
✅ Whether students truly understand (not just memorize)
✅ Whether they can reason geometrically
✅ Whether they grasp cultural connections
✅ Whether they can apply learning beyond the lesson
✅ Whether they think like mathematicians and designers

## IMPLEMENTING THE HIERARCHY IN ALL MATERIALS:

### In eTextbook Chapter:

**Investigation Questions (opening):**
- Start with 1 WHAT question
- Include 2-3 WHY/WHY NOT or WHAT IF questions as driving questions

**Geometric Thinking Boxes (throughout myth):**
- Mix of HOW and WHY questions
- Example: "WHY would chaos be represented by scattered shapes?"

**Your Turn Investigation Prompts (in Myth in Art section):**
- Start: WHAT do you notice?
- Middle: HOW is this constructed?
- End: WHY this choice? WHAT IF we changed it?

**Practice Problems (Mathematical Connections section):**
- WHAT: Identify the geometric element
- HOW: Calculate/measure/construct
- WHY: Explain the mathematical reasoning
- WHAT IF: Modify the design - what changes mathematically?

**Activity Instructions:**
- HOW to create (procedural)
- WHY these geometric choices matter (design decisions)
- WHAT IF explorations in extensions

**Reflection (end of chapter):**
- Mostly WHY and WHAT IF questions
- Example: "WHY did geometric precision matter to Mesopotamians?"
- Example: "WHAT IF modern architects used these principles?"

### In Slide Deck (Teacher Notes):

Every slide with a discussion prompt must indicate question level:

**Slide 12 - [Image of Ishtar with 8-pointed star]**
*Teacher Notes:*
- WHAT question: "What shape is Ishtar's symbol?" (quick check)
- HOW question: "How many points of symmetry does this have?"
- 💰 WHY question: "Why 8 points specifically? Why not 6 or 10?" (spend time here)
- 💰 WHAT IF question: "What if Ishtar had 12 points - how would that change the meaning?" (extend if time)

**Timing guidance:** Spend 30 seconds on WHAT, 1 minute on HOW, 3-5 minutes on WHY/WHAT IF

---

### In Teacher Script:

**Scripted Questions Must Include Level Indicators:**

```
[Slide 15] - [Time 12:00-17:00]

TEACHER ASKS (WHAT - quick check):
"What geometric shape do you see repeated in this border pattern?"

[Wait for responses - 30 seconds]

TEACHER ASKS (HOW - procedural):
"How is this pattern created? What transformations do you see?"

[Students think-pair-share - 1 minute]

TEACHER ASKS (💰 WHY - MONEY QUESTION):
"Why do you think the Mesopotamians chose symmetrical patterns for religious buildings? Why not random or asymmetrical designs?"

[This is the important question - give 3-5 minutes for discussion]

ANTICIPATED RESPONSES:
- "Symmetry looks more organized" → Push further: "Why does organization matter for a temple?"
- "It's prettier" → Challenge: "What makes symmetry beautiful? Is there a cultural reason?"
- "To show the gods are organized/powerful" → YES! Expand on this

FOLLOW-UP (💰 WHAT IF):
"What if we were designing a temple for a chaos god - what geometric choices would we make? Why?"
```

### In Student Worksheet:

**Question Progression Example:**

**Investigating the Rosette Pattern:**

1. **WHAT:** Draw the rosette you see in the artifact on slide 18. Label the shapes.

2. **HOW:** How many lines of symmetry does this rosette have? Show them on your drawing.

3. **WHY:** 💰 Why do you think the Sumerians used 6-fold symmetry for this design? Why not 4-fold or 8-fold?
   [Lines for written response]

4. **WHAT IF:** 💰 What if you designed a rosette for a different purpose - maybe representing water instead of a flower? How would you change the geometric pattern? Why?
   [Space for sketch and explanation]

**Grading Note:** Questions 3 and 4 are where understanding is truly assessed. Questions 1-2 are baseline checks.

### In Activity Rubrics:

**Traditional Rubric Mistake:**
❌ Categories like "Neatness," "Followed directions," "Effort"

**Correct Rubric - Emphasizes Reasoning:**

| Category | Below Standard | Approaching | Meeting | Exceeding |
|----------|---------------|-------------|---------|-----------|
| **Geometric Accuracy** (HOW) | Shapes incorrect or measurements off by >10% | Minor errors in construction | All geometric elements accurate | All elements accurate + additional geometric complexity |
| **Mathematical Reasoning** (WHY) 💰 | Cannot explain geometric choices | Partial explanation of why patterns work | Clearly explains why geometric choices were made mathematically | Explains multiple geometric relationships and their interdependence |
| **Cultural Connection** (WHY) 💰 | No connection to Mesopotamian beliefs | Mentions myth but doesn't connect to geometry | Clearly connects geometric choices to mythological meaning | Demonstrates sophisticated understanding of how belief systems inspired design |
| **Creative Application** (WHAT IF) 💰 | Copied example without modification | Minor variation from example | Original design applying lesson concepts | Original design + explores "what if" variations with reasoning |

**Point Distribution:**
- HOW category: 20%
- WHY categories: 50% (combined)
- WHAT IF category: 30%

**The majority of points are in the money questions.**

## VISUAL REQUIREMENTS FOR ALL SLIDES - MANDATORY

### EVERY SLIDE MUST HAVE VISUAL CONTENT

**Rule:** No slide can be text-only. Every single slide requires visual elements.

**Why:** The learning objectives are GEOMETRIC and VISUAL. Students are learning to see, analyze, and create visual patterns. Text alone cannot teach visual thinking.

---

### MINIMUM VISUAL REQUIREMENTS:

**Option 1: Full Image**
- Authentic Mesopotamian artifact fills the slide
- Text minimal or overlaid
- Image is the primary content

**Option 2: Image + Text**
- Large image (takes up at least 50% of slide)
- Text supplements the visual
- Image and text work together

**Option 3: Multiple Images**
- 2-6 smaller images
- Comparison, sequence, or examples
- Each image clearly visible

**Option 4: Diagram/Overlay**
- Geometric diagram showing concept
- Can be overlaid on artifact photo
- Labeled shapes, angles, measurements visible

**Option 5: Visual Background (Minimum Acceptable)**
- If slide must be text-heavy (rare)
- Use translucent geometric pattern as background
- Pattern relates to lesson content
- Example: Faded rosette pattern behind text
- Still provides visual connection

---

### VISUAL QUALITY STANDARDS:

**For Artifact Images:**
- Minimum resolution: 1200px width
- High quality (no pixelation when projected)
- Properly cropped (focus on relevant elements)
- Color-corrected (accurate representation)
- Visible when projected from back of room

**For Geometric Overlays:**
- High contrast with background (bright color on image)
- Line weight minimum 3pt (visible when projected)
- Labels font size 18+ 
- Arrows/pointers clear
- Not cluttered (limit to 3-5 labeled elements per slide)

**For Diagrams:**
- Clean, professional appearance
- Accurate geometric construction
- Sufficient size (not tiny diagrams)
- Color-coded meaningfully
- Legends included if using multiple colors

**For Backgrounds:**
- Subtle (don't compete with foreground content)
- Translucent (50-70% opacity)
- Pattern recognizable but not distracting
- Consistent across related slides

---

### MULTIPLE IMAGES STRATEGY:

**When to Use Multiple Images:**
- Comparing different artifacts showing same deity
- Showing pattern evolution across time periods
- Demonstrating steps in construction
- Before/after overlays
- Student work examples

**Layout Guidelines for Multiple Images:**

**2 Images:**
- Side-by-side comparison
- 50/50 split or 60/40 if one is primary
- Clear labels: "Example 1" / "Example 2" or "Sumerian Period" / "Babylonian Period"

**3 Images:**
- Horizontal row
- Triangular arrangement (1 top, 2 bottom)
- Sequential arrangement (1→2→3 flow)

**4 Images:**
- 2x2 grid
- Quadrant layout
- Each clearly labeled

**5-6 Images:**
- Gallery layout
- One primary (larger) + supporting images (smaller)
- Grid with some images spanning more space

**Critical:** Each image must be large enough to see details when projected

### RELATING EVERY SLIDE TO GEOMETRIC LEARNING OBJECTIVES:

**Since every learning objective is geometric/visual, every slide must show geometry.**

**Text Slide Examples - BEFORE/AFTER:**

**❌ WRONG - Text Only:**
```
Slide: "The Myth of Inanna"
- Inanna was the goddess of love and war
- She descended to the underworld
- She passed through seven gates
- She was transformed
```

**✅ CORRECT - Text + Visual:**
```
Slide: "The Myth of Inanna" 
[Full-screen image: Inanna at the underworld gate with 8-pointed star symbol prominent]
- Goddess of love & war
- Descended through 7 gates → [small diagram showing 7-pointed star]
- 8-pointed star = her divine symbol → [star with geometric overlay showing 8-fold symmetry, 45° angles labeled]
```

---

**❌ WRONG - Definition Slide:**
```
Slide: "Rotational Symmetry"
Definition: "A shape has rotational symmetry if it can be rotated less than 360° and look identical."
```

**✅ CORRECT - Visual Definition:**
```
Slide: "Rotational Symmetry"
[Background: Large Mesopotamian rosette pattern]
[Center: Animated or overlaid diagram showing rosette rotating 60° four times]
[Text overlay with transparency box]: "Rotational symmetry: looks identical after rotation"
[Arrows showing 60° rotation angles]
```

---

**❌ WRONG - Instructions:**
```
Slide: "Activity 1 Instructions"
1. Use your compass to draw a circle
2. Mark six points around the circle
3. Connect the points
4. Add decorative elements
```

**✅ CORRECT - Visual Instructions:**
```
Slide: "Activity 1: Create a Rosette"
[Four images showing steps]:
Image 1: Compass on paper creating circle [labeled "Step 1"]
Image 2: Six points marked with protractor showing 60° [labeled "Step 2: 360° ÷ 6 = 60°"]
Image 3: Points connected forming pattern [labeled "Step 3"]
Image 4: Completed decorated rosette [labeled "Step 4"]
[Brief text under each image, but images are primary]
```

### SPECIAL SLIDE TYPES - VISUAL REQUIREMENTS:

**Title Slides:**
- Beautiful artifact image as background
- Translucent overlay box for text
- Title + subtitle visible but image prominent

**Transition Slides:**
- Bridge between sections
- Relevant image or pattern
- Minimal text ("Now let's investigate...")

**Discussion Slides:**
- Provocative image that prompts the question
- Question text doesn't hide image details
- Multiple images if comparing/contrasting

**Assessment Slides:**
- Image of what students are analyzing
- Question text minimal
- Visual is what students assess

**Summary Slides:**
- Gallery of key images from lesson
- Small text bullets
- Visual reminder of journey

## QUALITY CHECK QUESTIONS:

Before finalizing any material, ask:

**For Assessment Questions:**
1. Do I have mostly WHY/WHY NOT and WHAT IF questions?
2. Are WHAT and HOW questions just quick baseline checks?
3. Do my money questions require reasoning and justification?
4. Would students need to think deeply to answer the important questions?

**For Visuals:**
1. Does every slide have meaningful visual content?
2. Can students learn geometry by looking at the images?
3. Would removing images make the lesson impossible to teach?
4. Are geometric elements clearly visible?
5. Do visuals support or distract from learning?

---

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID:

**Assessment Mistakes:**
❌ All questions at WHAT level
❌ Spending most time on low-level questions
❌ Not allowing time for deep discussion of WHY
❌ Treating all questions as equally important
❌ Grading effort instead of reasoning

**Visual Mistakes:**
❌ Text-only slides
❌ Tiny images (can't see details)
❌ Too many images (cluttered, nothing visible)
❌ Generic geometric diagrams (not connected to Mesopotamian context)
❌ Low-resolution pixelated images
❌ Overlays that obscure instead of clarify
❌ Decorative images (not teaching geometric concepts)

---

## THE BOTTOM LINE:

**Assessment:** The learning happens in WHY and WHAT IF. Don't waste time over-assessing WHAT and HOW. Get to the money questions.

**Visuals:** Geometry IS visual. Every slide must show geometry. No exceptions.

**Together:** Students should see geometric patterns, analyze them visually, and reason about WHY and WHAT IF using visual evidence.

---

**This is the foundation of quality Mesopotamian geometry curriculum. No compromises.**
