# SECTION 2: GEOMETRIC ELEMENT + MYTH CONNECTION
## How the Circle Appears in Shamash's Story (8 minutes)

### The Circle in Shamash's Identity

Shamash, the Mesopotamian sun god, embodies circular geometry through every aspect of his divine nature. The circle is not merely decorative—it is fundamental to understanding who Shamash is and what he represents to ancient Mesopotamians.

**Primary Circular Elements:**

1. **The Sun Disk Itself** - Shamash's most recognizable symbol is the solar disk, always depicted as a perfect circle. This disk appears on his chest in reliefs, as a medallion he holds, or as the sun itself in the sky. The circle represents the sun's form as observed from Earth—a luminous disk with no beginning and no end.

2. **The Daily Path** - Shamash's journey across the sky traces an arc, which is a section of a circle. Ancient Mesopotamians understood that the sun's path, when extended, would form a complete circle around the Earth. This circular path is constant and predictable—the foundation of their timekeeping.

3. **Equal Distance** - As the sun god, Shamash shines on all people equally. From the center point of the sun, light radiates outward in all directions. Every person standing on Earth is theoretically the same distance from this center point—like points on the circumference of a circle are all equal distance from the center.

4. **The Rays** - Sun rays emanate from Shamash's disk in straight lines radiating from a central point, like the radii of a circle. Ancient artists depicted 4, 8, 12, or 16 rays, always evenly spaced, demonstrating their understanding of angular division.

### Why the Circle Represents Justice

Shamash was not only the sun god but also the god of justice. The ancient Mesopotamians saw a profound connection between circular geometry and justice:

- **No Beginning, No End**: A circle has no start or finish, symbolizing eternal law that exists before and after human rulers
- **Equal Distance from Center**: All points on a circle's edge are the same distance from the center, just as all people are equal before the law
- **Perfect Symmetry**: A circle looks identical from any viewing angle, representing impartial justice that treats everyone the same
- **Completeness**: A circle encloses space perfectly with no gaps, symbolizing comprehensive justice that leaves nothing out

The Code of Hammurabi, one of the oldest law codes, shows Shamash presenting the laws to King Hammurabi. Shamash sits on a throne, holding measuring tools, with his sun disk prominently displayed—geometry and justice unified in one image.

### Geometric Properties Demonstrated

The Shamash myth teaches several geometric principles through metaphor:

**Center Point**: Shamash represents the center—the origin from which light and justice radiate. In geometry, the center is the defining feature of a circle, the single point from which all measurements are taken.

**Radius (Constant Distance)**: The distance from Shamash (center) to any point he illuminates remains constant during any moment of observation. This is the definition of radius—the distance from center to circumference that never changes.

**Diameter**: When Shamash rises in the east and sets in the west, he travels across the full width of the sky—crossing through the center point, creating a diameter.

**Circumference**: His complete path, if we could see the entire circle including the underground portion, forms the circumference—the complete distance around the circle.

**Rotational Symmetry**: The sun disk appears the same no matter which direction you rotate it, demonstrating infinite rotational symmetry—a unique property of circles.

### Metaphorical Geometry in Shamash's Attributes

When ancient Mesopotamians said "Shamash sees all," they were describing a geometric principle. From an elevated center point, vision extends equally in all directions—forming a circle of sight. The higher the viewpoint (midday sun at highest point), the larger the circle of vision.

When they said "Shamash's light reaches everyone equally," they described the radius property—from center to circumference, the distance is constant, so light travels the same distance to reach each person.

When they described his path as "eternal and unchanging," they recognized the mathematical perfection of circular motion—planets and sun moving in predictable circular paths that could be calculated and predicted.

### Technique Analysis: How Did They Create Perfect Circles?

Ancient Mesopotamian artisans did not have modern compasses, rulers, or measuring tools, yet they created remarkably precise circular forms. Archaeological evidence and experimental archaeology suggest several methods:

**Method 1: The Stake and String Compass**
- Drive a wooden stake into ground (or hold firmly on work surface)
- Tie string to stake at fixed length (= radius)
- Tie marking tool (reed stylus, sharp stone) to other end of string
- Keep string taut and walk/move marking tool around stake
- Result: Perfect circle with radius = string length

**Method 2: Potter's Wheel (for ceramics)**
- Rotating wheel ensures circular symmetry
- Clay centered exactly on wheel center
- As wheel spins, any tool held steady creates perfect circle
- Mesopotamians used potter's wheels from Ubaid period onward (~5500 BCE)

**Method 3: Template and Tracing**
- Create one perfect circle using Method 1
- Cut template from wood, leather, or clay
- Trace around template for subsequent circles
- All circles identical to original

**Method 4: Rolling Measurement**
- For circular weights: grind stone while rotating it
- Check symmetry by rolling - imperfect circles wobble
- Continue grinding until roll is smooth
- Final test: roll across flat surface; straight line = perfect circle

### Step-by-Step Instructions

#### **STEP 1: Prepare Your Paper and Find the Center** (1 minute)

**Action**: Position your paper horizontally (landscape orientation). Use your ruler to measure and mark the center of the paper.

**Detailed Process**:
1. Place your 9" × 12" paper horizontally on your desk
2. Use ruler to measure paper width: 12 inches
3. Divide by 2: 12 ÷ 2 = 6 inches
4. Measure 6 inches from the left edge, make a small pencil mark
5. Use ruler to measure paper height: 9 inches
6. Divide by 2: 9 ÷ 2 = 4.5 inches
7. Measure 4.5 inches from the bottom edge, make a small pencil mark where it crosses the 6-inch mark

**What It Should Look Like**: You should have a small, light pencil mark in the center of your paper where the two measurements cross.

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Don't press too hard with pencil—center mark should be light
- Don't skip finding center; guessing leads to off-center circles

#### **STEP 2: Set Your Compass to 3-Inch Radius** (1 minute)

**Action**: Adjust your compass so the distance from the point to the pencil is exactly 3 inches.

**Detailed Process**:
1. Place your ruler flat on the desk
2. Put the compass point at the "0" mark on the ruler
3. Open the compass arms until the pencil tip reaches the "3" mark
4. Tighten the compass screw/joint so it won't change
5. Double-check by measuring again: point at 0, pencil at 3

**What It Should Look Like**: The compass should open to exactly 3 inches and hold that measurement without slipping.

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Compass that's too loose will change size while drawing
- Measuring from wrong part of compass point
- Forgetting to tighten compass

#### **STEP 3: Draw Your Circle** (1-2 minutes)

**Action**: Place the compass point on your center mark and rotate carefully to draw a perfect circle.

**Detailed Process**:
1. Hold compass by the top knob with your dominant hand
2. Place the metal point firmly on your center mark
3. Tilt the compass slightly (about 20° from vertical)
4. Keep point pressed down (don't let it slip!)
5. Slowly rotate the pencil end around in one smooth motion
6. Complete the full circle, ending where you started
7. Lift the compass straight up when finished

**What It Should Look Like**: A perfect circle with no gaps, overlaps, or wobbly sections. The center mark should still be visible with a small indent from the compass point.

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Letting the compass point slip (causes distorted circle)
- Stopping mid-circle to adjust (creates bumps)
- Pressing too hard with pencil (makes thick, dark line)
- Lifting the paper edge (causes wobbly line)

#### **STEP 4: Mark 12 Evenly-Spaced Points on Circle** (2-3 minutes)

**Action**: Divide your circle into 12 equal sections by marking points around the circumference.

**Detailed Process**:
1. Imagine your circle is a clock face
2. Mark the "12 o'clock" position (top of circle) with small pencil dot
3. Mark the "6 o'clock" position (bottom) directly opposite
4. Mark the "3 o'clock" position (right side)
5. Mark the "9 o'clock" position (left side)
6. Now you have 4 points dividing circle into quarters
7. Mark points halfway between 12 and 3 (1 o'clock and 2 o'clock positions)
8. Mark points halfway between 3 and 6 (4 o'clock and 5 o'clock)
9. Mark points halfway between 6 and 9 (7 o'clock and 8 o'clock)
10. Mark points halfway between 9 and 12 (10 o'clock and 11 o'clock)
11. Total: 12 points evenly spaced around circle

**Math Connection**: 360° ÷ 12 points = 30° between each point

**What It Should Look Like**: 12 small, evenly-spaced dots around your circle's edge, like numbers on a clock.

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Spacing points by eye without using clock method (leads to uneven rays)
