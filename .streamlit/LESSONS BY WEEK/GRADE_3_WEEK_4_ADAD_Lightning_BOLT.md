## A4: Geometric Analysis - Discovering Sharp Angle Magic
**Duration:** 8-10 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will explore acute angles and understand how sharp corners create powerful, cutting shapes.

### Angle Detective Work
Using paper shapes, protractors, and corner testers, students will be angle detectives!

**Angle Types to Find:**
- **Sharp Angles (Acute):** Smaller than a square corner (less than 90°) - like a slice of pizza
- **Square Angles (Right):** Exactly like the corner of a square or rectangle (90°)
- **Wide Angles (Obtuse):** Bigger than a square corner (more than 90°) - like opening a book wide

### Hands-On Angle Exploration
**Activity 1: Paper Corner Testing**
1. Give students paper squares
2. Fold them to make different angle sizes
3. Compare their folded angles to square corners (90°)
4. Identify which angles are sharper (acute) and which are wider (obtuse)

**Activity 2: Lightning Bolt Building**
1. Use straws or craft sticks to build lightning bolt shapes
2. Make sure every corner is a sharp angle (acute)
3. Try building with wide angles - does it still look like lightning?
4. Count how many sharp angles are in each lightning bolt

**Activity 3: Sharp Angle Hunt**
Find sharp angles around the classroom:
- Pencil points, scissors, corners of triangles
- Compare to square corners of books and desks
- Look for places where sharp angles help tools work better

### Amazing Discoveries
**What Our Angle Detectives Find:**
- Sharp angles (acute angles) are less than 90 degrees
- Lightning bolts are made entirely of sharp angles
- Sharp angles help tools cut, poke, and break through things
- The sharper the angle, the more "cutting power" it has

**Decomposition Activity:** Break down what makes a lightning bolt:
- Multiple line segments (straight pieces)
- Sharp angle connections (acute angles at every turn)
- Zigzag pattern (changes direction suddenly)

**Artifacts Required:**
- Paper squares for angle folding
- Straws or craft sticks for building lightning bolts
- Simple protractors or angle guides
- Examples of acute, right, and obtuse angles around the classroom
