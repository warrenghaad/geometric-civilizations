## DAY A: METAPHOR

**A2: SEL + Metaphor (Connection)**

Fluids flow—they do not maintain fixed shape but conform to containers and channels, moving continuously from higher to lower potential. This flowing nature enables transport: water carries nutrients to cells, silt to floodplains, boats to destinations. Flow connects separated locations, enabling exchange and distribution.

Water's flow can be chaotic (turbulent rapids, crashing waves) or ordered (smooth laminar streams, coordinated wave trains). The difference depends on conditions: velocity, viscosity, channel geometry, and disturbances. Under appropriate conditions, water organizes into remarkably regular patterns—parallel waves, steady currents, predictable oscillations.

Ea (Sumerian: Enki) governed fresh water—the life-sustaining water of rivers, springs, and the subterranean aquifer (Abzu). Unlike the chaotic saltwater ocean (Tiamat's domain), Ea's fresh water represented ordered, beneficial flow. Rivers followed channels; springs emerged at predictable locations; the Abzu supplied wells reliably.

Ea was also god of wisdom and magic—domains requiring ordered thought and systematic procedure. The association between water and wisdom may reflect observation: water flows along paths of least resistance, finding optimal routes without deliberation. Wisdom, analogously, finds efficient solutions. Ea's clever indirect approaches (speaking to walls knowing others would overhear, finding loopholes in divine edicts) parallel water's indirect navigation around obstacles.

The water streams symbol—parallel wavy lines undulating in phase—represents coordinated flow. Multiple streams moving together, peaks and troughs aligned, express collective order within fluid motion.

**A3: Symbolic Chain (Iconographic Art)**

WATER STREAMS → PARALLEL WAVES → COORDINATED FLOW → EA → ORDERED BENEFICIAL MOVEMENT

The water streams symbol depicts sinusoidal or quasi-sinusoidal curves arranged in parallel, their oscillations synchronized.

**Wave parameters:**

A sinusoidal wave can be described by:

y(x,t) = A × sin(kx - ωt + φ)

where:

- A = amplitude (maximum displacement from centerline)
- k = wave number = 2π/λ (spatial frequency)
- λ = wavelength (distance between successive peaks)
- ω = angular frequency = 2πf (temporal frequency)
- f = frequency (cycles per unit time)
- φ = phase (initial offset)
- x = position along wave propagation direction
- t = time

For the static water streams symbol (representing a snapshot), time is fixed, giving:

y(x) = A × sin(kx + φ₀)

where φ₀ = φ - ωt₀ is the phase at the depicted instant.

**Parallel wave parameters:**

When multiple waves are drawn in parallel (vertically offset), they may share:

- Same wavelength λ (peaks vertically aligned)
- Same amplitude A (equal oscillation magnitude)
- Same phase φ (peaks occur at same x positions)

This phase coherence—peaks aligned across all streams—represents coordinated flow. Water moving through parallel channels in phase indicates unified, ordered motion.

**Wavelength-amplitude ratio:**

The visual character of the wave depends on the ratio λ/A:

- λ/A >> 1: gentle undulations (nearly straight)
- λ/A ≈ 4-8: typical wave appearance
- λ/A << 1: tight oscillations (nearly zigzag)

Mesopotamian water stream symbols typically show λ/A ≈ 4-6, producing recognizably wavy but not extreme forms.

**A4: Deity Iconography (Institutionalization)**

Ea's iconography combines water imagery with symbols of wisdom and fertility.

**Water streams from shoulders:**

In characteristic depictions, streams of water flow from Ea's shoulders or from a vessel he holds. These streams often contain fish, emphasizing the life-sustaining nature of fresh water. The parallel streams, rendered as wavy lines, identify the figure as Ea.

The shoulder-streams may represent rivers (Tigris and Euphrates) or springs emerging from the divine body. The visual motif connects the god physically to his domain—water literally flows from him.

**The Goat-Fish (Suhurmaššu):**

Ea's associated creature is the suhurmaššu—a hybrid with goat's forequarters and fish's tail. This composite represents the liminal quality of Ea's domain: between land (goat) and water (fish), between surface and depths.

The goat-fish appears on kudurru boundary stones as Ea's symbol and in astronomical contexts associated with the constellation Capricornus.

**The Abzu:**

Ea dwelt in the Abzu (Akkadian: Apsû)—the freshwater ocean believed to exist beneath the earth. Springs, wells, and rivers were understood as Abzu water reaching the surface. Ea's temple at Eridu was called E-abzu ("House of the Abzu"), architecturally representing access to this subterranean realm.

The Abzu was not merely water but the source of wisdom and magical power. Ea's epithets include "lord of the Abzu" and "king of the Abzu," emphasizing rulership over this cosmic reservoir.

**Ea's character:**

Mythological narratives portray Ea as clever, indirect, and often sympathetic to humanity:

- In the Atrahasis epic, when gods decide to destroy humanity by flood, Ea warns the hero by speaking to a reed wall (technically not telling a human directly, circumventing the divine decree)
- In Inanna's Descent, Ea creates beings from dirt under his fingernails to rescue Inanna from the underworld
- In various incantation texts, Ea provides the magical knowledge that enables healing and protection

This trickster-like wisdom—solving problems through cleverness rather than force—distinguishes Ea from more martial deities.

**A5: Material Culture (Process)**

Ea's cult centered at Eridu, one of the oldest continuously occupied sacred sites in Mesopotamia. Water-related rituals and incantations invoked Ea throughout Mesopotamian religion.

**Eridu and the E-abzu Temple:**

Eridu (modern Tell Abu Shahrain, Iraq) was considered the first city in Sumerian tradition. The Sumerian King List begins: "When kingship descended from heaven, kingship was in Eridu."

The temple sequence at Eridu spans approximately 5400-2000 BCE, with 18-20 successive building phases on the same location. This continuity—unmatched at other Mesopotamian sites—suggests Eridu's exceptional sanctity.

The earliest temples (Ubaid period, circa 5400-4500 BCE) were small single-room structures. Later temples grew larger and more elaborate, eventually including the E-abzu complex with multiple courts and chambers.

Excavations (1946-1949 by Fuad Safar and Seton Lloyd) revealed:

- Fish bones in temple offerings (connecting to Ea's aquatic domain)
- Ritual installations for water
- Continuous architectural tradition across millennia

**Ritual purification:**

Water played central roles in Mesopotamian ritual:

- Washing of cult statues and sacred objects
- Purification of persons entering sacred spaces
- Mouth-washing ceremonies (mīs pî) that animated cult statues
- Healing rituals involving water from the Abzu

These rituals conceptually accessed Ea's domain—bringing Abzu water (or its symbolic equivalent) into contact with persons or objects requiring purification or consecration.

**Incantations and magic:**

Ea was the ultimate source of magical knowledge. Incantation texts frequently invoke Ea (often together with his son Asalluhi/Marduk) as the authority behind curative spells:

"Ea has heard of your suffering; Ea, lord of the deep, has spoken the spell; Ea has given his word for your healing."

The incantation priest (āšipu) acted as Ea's agent, applying divine knowledge to earthly problems. Water often featured in the accompanying rituals—sprinkled, poured, or used for washing.

**A6: Art Technique (Process)**

Constructing wave forms requires understanding sinusoidal curves and their geometric properties.

**Sinusoidal curve construction:**

A sine curve can be constructed point-by-point:

1. Draw horizontal axis (x) and vertical axis (y)
2. Mark wavelength divisions along x: 0, λ/4, λ/2, 3λ/4, λ, 5λ/4, ...
3. Calculate y values:
    - At x = 0: y = A sin(0) = 0
    - At x = λ/4: y = A sin(π/2) = A
    - At x = λ/2: y = A sin(π) = 0
    - At x = 3λ/4: y = A sin(3π/2) = -A
    - At x = λ: y = A sin(2π) = 0
4. Plot points and connect with smooth curve
5. Extend pattern for multiple wavelengths

**Circle-to-sine relationship:**

The sine function relates to circular motion. For a point moving around a circle of radius A:

- If the point's angle from horizontal is θ
- The vertical position is y = A sin(θ)
- As θ increases uniformly (constant angular velocity), y traces a sine curve

This relationship enables construction:

1. Draw circle of radius A
2. Divide circumference into equal angular segments (e.g., every 30°)
3. Project each point horizontally onto a line
4. Space projections equally along horizontal axis
5. The projected points trace a sine curve

**Parallel wave construction:**

For water stream symbols with n parallel waves:

1. Construct first sinusoidal curve with chosen λ and A
2. Offset vertically by spacing distance d
3. Repeat the same curve (same λ, A, phase)
4. Continue for n waves total

Phase alignment: peaks of all waves occur at the same x positions Consistent spacing: vertical distance d is constant between curves

**Smooth curve technique:**

For freehand rendering:

- Mark peak and trough positions on parallel guidelines
- Connect with smooth curves passing through marked points
- Maintain consistent curvature (avoid flat spots or sharp corners)
- Compare wavelengths across the drawing for consistency

**B2: Math Proof (Proof)**

Water flow in open channels follows predictable patterns governed by channel geometry. The hydraulic radius quantifies the relationship between channel shape and flow efficiency.

**Velocity profile in channels:**

Water flowing through a channel experiences friction at the channel walls and bed. This friction slows water near the boundaries while water in the center flows faster.

For laminar flow in a wide rectangular channel, the velocity profile is approximately parabolic:

v(z) = v_max × (1 - (z/d)²)

where:

- v(z) = velocity at height z above the bed
- v_max = maximum velocity (at the surface, z = d)
- d = water depth

For circular pipes with laminar flow:

v(r) = v_max × (1 - (r/R)²)

where r is distance from the pipe center and R is pipe radius.

This parabolic profile means the average velocity is:

v_avg = (2/3) × v_max (for wide rectangular channel) v_avg = (1/2) × v_max (for circular pipe)

**Hydraulic radius:**

The hydraulic radius R_h is defined as:

R_h = A / P

where:

- A = cross-sectional flow area
- P = wetted perimeter (length of channel surface in contact with water)

This ratio measures flow efficiency: larger R_h means less boundary friction per unit flow area.

**Examples:**

Rectangular channel (width w, depth d):

- A = w × d
- P = w + 2d (bed plus two sides)
- R_h = (w × d) / (w + 2d)

For w = 2m, d = 0.5m:

- A = 1.0 m²
- P = 2 + 1 = 3 m
- R_h = 1.0 / 3 = 0.333 m

Semicircular channel (radius r):

- A = (1/2)πr²
- P = πr (curved wall only; water surface is not wetted perimeter)
- R_h = [(1/2)πr²] / [πr] = r/2

For r = 0.5m:

- A = 0.393 m²
- P = 1.571 m
- R_h = 0.25 m

**Optimal channel shape:**

Among channels of equal cross-sectional area, the semicircle has the maximum hydraulic radius, making it theoretically optimal for flow efficiency. This is because the circle minimizes perimeter for given area (isoperimetric theorem), and the semicircle applies this to open channels.

However, semicircular channels are difficult to construct and maintain. Trapezoidal channels offer a practical compromise between efficiency and constructability.

**B3: Transformation (Operation)**

Channel flow involves transformations of energy, velocity, and cross-sectional parameters along the flow path.

**Continuity equation:**

For incompressible flow (water), mass conservation requires:

A₁ × v₁ = A₂ × v₂ = Q (constant)

where:

- A₁, A₂ = cross-sectional areas at positions 1 and 2
- v₁, v₂ = average velocities at positions 1 and 2
- Q = volumetric flow rate (constant along channel)

This means: when a channel narrows, velocity increases; when it widens, velocity decreases.

Example: A canal narrows from w₁ = 4m to w₂ = 2m while maintaining the same depth d = 0.5m.

A₁ = 4 × 0.5 = 2 m² A₂ = 2 × 0.5 = 1 m²

If v₁ = 0.5 m/s: v₂ = (A₁/A₂) × v₁ = (2/1) × 0.5 = 1.0 m/s

The velocity doubles when the area halves.

**Energy transformation (Bernoulli principle):**

For ideal flow, total energy per unit volume is conserved:

P + (1/2)ρv² + ρgh = constant

where:

- P = pressure
- ρ = water density (≈ 1000 kg/m³)
- v = velocity
- g = gravitational acceleration (9.81 m/s²)
- h = elevation

For open channel flow, pressure at the surface is atmospheric (constant), so the relevant form is:

(1/2)v² + gh = constant (per unit mass)

This means: as water speeds up (higher v), its surface must drop (lower h) to conserve energy. This is visible at channel constrictions—the water surface dips where flow accelerates.

**Flow rate calculation:**

The Manning equation relates flow rate to channel geometry and slope:

v = (1/n) × R_h^(2/3) × S^(1/2)

where:

- n = Manning's roughness coefficient (dimensionless)
- R_h = hydraulic radius
- S = channel slope (elevation drop / horizontal distance)

Typical n values:

- Smooth concrete: n ≈ 0.012
- Earth channel, clean: n ≈ 0.022
- Earth channel, weedy: n ≈ 0.030
- Natural stream: n ≈ 0.035-0.050

Flow rate: Q = v × A

**B4: Mechanics (Result)**

Channel hydraulics determines irrigation system design. Understanding these principles explains Mesopotamian engineering choices and modern practices.

**Flow regime classification:**

The Reynolds number Re classifies flow as laminar or turbulent:

Re = (ρ × v × D_h) / μ = (v × D_h) / ν

where:

- ρ = fluid density
- v = velocity
- D_h = hydraulic diameter = 4 × R_h
- μ = dynamic viscosity
- ν = kinematic viscosity (for water at 20°C, ν ≈ 1.0 × 10⁻⁶ m²/s)

Flow regimes:

- Re < 500: Laminar (smooth, parallel streamlines)
- 500 < Re < 2000: Transitional
- Re > 2000: Turbulent (chaotic, mixing)

Most open channel flows are turbulent (high Re due to large dimensions and moderate velocities).

Example: Channel with R_h = 0.33m (D_h = 1.32m), v = 0.6 m/s: Re = (0.6 × 1.32) / (1.0 × 10⁻⁶) = 792,000

This extremely high Re indicates fully turbulent flow.

**Meander formation:**

In natural channels, flow around bends creates velocity asymmetry:

- Outside of bend: longer path, higher velocity
- Inside of bend: shorter path, lower velocity

Higher velocity causes erosion (outside bank retreats). Lower velocity causes deposition (inside bank advances). This feedback amplifies curves—small bends grow into large meanders.

Mesopotamian canals required maintenance to prevent meandering. Straight channels were easier to manage but required active intervention against natural tendencies.

**Sedimentation:**

Rivers carry suspended sediment. When velocity decreases (channel widens, slope decreases), sediment settles. Mesopotamian canals required regular dredging to remove accumulated silt.

The critical velocity v_c for sediment transport depends on particle size d:

v_c ≈ √(8g × d × (ρ_s - ρ_w) / (f × ρ_w))

where:

- g = gravitational acceleration
- d = particle diameter
- ρ_s = sediment density (≈ 2650 kg/m³ for sand)
- ρ_w = water density (≈ 1000 kg/m³)
- f = friction factor

For fine sand (d = 0.2 mm): v_c ≈ 0.2-0.3 m/s For coarse sand (d = 1 mm): v_c ≈ 0.4-0.5 m/s

Maintaining velocity above v_c prevents deposition; allowing velocity to fall below v_c enables deliberate settling basins.

**B5: STEM History (Context)**

Mesopotamian irrigation represents one of humanity's earliest and most extensive hydraulic engineering achievements, developing over millennia in response to environmental challenges.

**Environmental context:**

Southern Mesopotamia (Sumer, Babylonia) receives approximately 100-200 mm annual rainfall—insufficient for rain-fed agriculture. The Tigris and Euphrates rivers, fed by precipitation in the Armenian highlands, provided water for irrigation.

The rivers present challenges:

- Flooding occurs in spring (April-June), after the growing season begins
- Low water occurs in late summer/fall, when crops need water
- Both rivers carry heavy sediment loads
- The flat terrain complicates drainage

Successful agriculture required storing spring floods, distributing water during low flow, managing sediment, and preventing waterlogging and salinization.

**Canal system development:**

Archaeological evidence for irrigation begins in the Ubaid period (circa 6000 BCE). By the Early Dynastic period (circa 2900-2350 BCE), major canal systems linked rivers to agricultural zones.

Canal hierarchy:

- Primary canals: 10-20 m wide, drawing directly from rivers
- Secondary canals: 5-10 m wide, distributing water to regions
- Tertiary canals: 1-3 m wide, serving village areas
- Field channels: < 1 m wide, delivering water to individual plots

Control structures included:

- Weirs: raised barriers regulating water levels
- Sluice gates: adjustable openings controlling flow
- Diversion dams: directing flow into canal intakes
- Drop structures: dissipating energy on steep gradients

**Administrative records:**

Cuneiform tablets document water management:

- Water allocation disputes and resolutions
- Labor quotas for canal construction and maintenance
- Crop yields correlated with irrigation adequacy
- Official positions for canal supervisors

The Code of Hammurabi (circa 1754 BCE) includes provisions on irrigation:

- Liability for farmers who flood neighbors' fields through negligence
- Obligations to maintain canal banks
- Compensation for crop damage from water mismanagement

**Salinization crisis:**

Irrigation without adequate drainage causes salinization. Water evaporates from fields, leaving dissolved salts behind. Accumulated salts reduce crop yields and eventually render land infertile.

Evidence suggests significant salinization in southern Mesopotamia by the late third millennium BCE:

- Shift from salt-sensitive wheat to salt-tolerant barley
- Declining yields documented in administrative records
- Northward shift of political power (Akkad, Babylon rather than Sumer)

This ancient ecological crisis offers lessons for modern irrigation management.

**B6: Invention Moment (Specific Breakthrough)**

**Core insight:** Channel shape determines flow efficiency through the hydraulic radius relationship—the ratio of flow area to wetted perimeter.

This insight, whether discovered empirically or theoretically, enabled optimization of canal design. Channels could be shaped to maximize water delivery per unit excavation and maintenance effort.

**Empirical discovery pathway:**

Mesopotamian engineers likely observed:

1. Wider channels with the same depth flow faster (less wall friction relative to flow)
2. Deeper channels with the same width also flow faster (same reason)
3. Smooth-lined channels flow faster than rough ones
4. Certain proportions work better than others for given conditions

Through trial, error, and accumulated experience across generations, practical guidelines emerged. These guidelines implicitly optimized hydraulic radius without explicit mathematical formulation.

**Modern formalization:**

The mathematical understanding of channel hydraulics developed mainly in the 18th-19th centuries CE:

- Antoine Chézy (1769): Flow velocity proportional to √(R_h × S)
- Robert Manning (1889): Refined formula v = (1/n) × R_h^(2/3) × S^(1/2)

These formulas confirmed and quantified what practical engineers had discovered empirically.

**Design optimization:**

For a trapezoidal channel (common practical form) with:

- Bottom width b
- Side slope z (horizontal:vertical ratio)
- Depth d

The optimal depth-to-width ratio for maximum hydraulic radius can be derived:

For given area A, the optimal trapezoidal section has: d/b ≈ 0.5 for vertical sides (z = 0, rectangular) d/b increases as z increases

The half-hexagon (z = 1/√3 ≈ 0.577) with depth d = √3 × b/2 is theoretically optimal among trapezoids.

**Mesopotamian achievement:**

Without calculus or explicit hydraulic theory, Mesopotamian engineers developed functional irrigation systems serving millions of hectares. Their empirical optimization approached theoretical optima for practical constraints (constructability, durability, maintainability).

The systems failed eventually—but not from poor hydraulic design. Salinization, political disruption, and maintenance failures caused collapse, not fundamental engineering errors.

Build three channels in the tray, each approximately 30 cm long, with the following cross-sections (all with equal area ≈ 4 cm²):

1. **Channel V (V-shaped):**

    - Depth: 2.83 cm
    - Width at top: 5.66 cm
    - Area: (1/2) × 5.66 × 2.83 ≈ 8 cm² [correction: for 4 cm², depth ≈ 2 cm, width ≈ 4 cm]
    - Let's recalculate: For 90° V, A = d², so d = 2 cm, top width = 4 cm, A = 4 cm²
    - P = 2 × d × √2 = 2 × 2 × 1.414 = 5.66 cm
    - R_h = 4 / 5.66 = 0.71 cm
2. **Channel U (semicircular):**
    
    - Semicircular cross-section
    - For A = 4 cm²: (1/2)πr² = 4, so r² = 8/π = 2.55, r = 1.60 cm
    - P = πr = 5.03 cm
    - R_h = 4 / 5.03 = 0.80 cm
3. **Channel R (rectangular, wide and shallow):**
    
    - Width: 4 cm, Depth: 1 cm
    - A = 4 × 1 = 4 cm²
    - P = 4 + 2 × 1 = 6 cm
    - R_h = 4 / 6 = 0.67 cm
