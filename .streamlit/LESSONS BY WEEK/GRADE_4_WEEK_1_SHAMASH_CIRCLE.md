## A3: Deity Introduction - Shamash, The Fair Judge
**Duration:** 6-7 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will understand how Shamash used circular motion to represent justice and fairness.

### Shamash's Daily Journey
Shamash was the sun god who judged all the people on Earth. Every day, he traveled across the sky in a perfect circle, watching everything that happened below. Because he traveled in a circle, he could see all parts of the world equally.

**Shamash's Special Powers:**
- **Fair Seeing:** His circular path let him watch over everyone equally
- **Equal Treatment:** His sunlight reached all people the same way
- **Daily Reliability:** He made the same circular journey every day
- **Just Decisions:** He gave good laws to help people live together peacefully

### The Circular Symbol
Shamash's symbol was a circle with rays coming out like sunbeams. This wasn't just a pretty picture - it showed how circles work:

**Why Circles Represent Fairness:**
- **Same Distance:** Every point on a circle is the exact same distance from the center
- **No Corners:** Circles don't have "best spots" or "worst spots" like squares do  
- **Equal Division:** You can divide a circle into equal parts for fair sharing
- **Endless:** Circles have no beginning or end, showing that fairness should last forever

**Interactive Question:** "If you were designing a symbol for fairness today, would you choose a circle or a square? Why?"

**Artifacts Required:**
- Large drawing of Shamash's sun disk symbol
- Props showing circular vs. square division problems
- Simple compass for drawing circles

## A4: Geometric Analysis - Circle Properties
**Duration:** 8-10 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will discover and measure the basic properties that make circles useful for solving problems.

### Exploring Real Circles
Using coins, plates, and other circular objects, students will investigate:

**Circle Parts to Discover:**
- **Center:** The exact middle point
- **Edge (circumference):** The line that goes all the way around
- **Radius:** The distance from center to edge (always the same!)
- **Diameter:** The distance straight across through the center

### Hands-On Investigation
**Measurement Activity:**
1. **Find the Center:** Fold a paper circle in half, then in half again. Where the creases cross is the center
2. **Check Equal Distance:** Use string to measure from center to edge at 6 different spots. Are they all the same?
3. **Compare Shapes:** Do the same measurements with squares and triangles. What's different?

**Problem-Solving Challenge:**
"You need to arrange 8 chairs so everyone can see a speaker equally well. Try arranging them in a circle, a square, and a straight line. Which works best?"

### Mathematical Discoveries
**What Students Find:**
- All radius measurements are equal (constant distance from center)
- Circles have no corners to create "bad" seats
- Equal spacing around a circle is easy to achieve
- Circles contain the most space for a given boundary

**Decomposition Activity:** Break down circular objects into their parts:
- Center point (where everything connects)
- Radius measurement (determines the size)  
- Curved edge (the boundary that curves equally)

**Artifacts Required:**
- Various circular objects for measurement
- String, rulers, paper circles
- Chairs or markers for arrangement activity
- Graph paper for recording measurements

## A6: Mathematical History - Numbers and Circles
**Duration:** 6-8 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will discover how circular measurements led to mathematical systems we still use.

### From Circles to Counting
Mesopotamians made important mathematical discoveries by studying circles that we still use today.

**Mathematical Innovations:**
1. **Circle Division:** They divided circles into 360 parts (degrees) - like slicing a pizza into 360 tiny slices
2. **Time Measurement:** They used circular sundials to divide days into hours
3. **Fair Sharing:** They figured out how to divide circles equally for any number of people
4. **Distance Around:** They discovered that the distance around a circle is always about 3 times the distance across

### Practical Problem-Solving
**Ancient Word Problems:**
1. "A potter needs to make 6 equal-sized drinking cups from one large circular piece of clay. How should he divide his circle?"
2. "A farmer wants to build a circular fence around his sheep. If the fence goes 20 steps across, how many steps will he walk around the whole circle?"

**Modern Connections:**
- Pizza shops still use circle division to make equal slices
- Clocks use circular measurement for hours and minutes
- Architects use circle math to design round buildings

### Pattern Recognition
**What Students Discover:**
- Dividing circles equally creates useful fractions
- Circular measurements help solve distance problems
- The same math works for tiny coins and huge buildings

**Artifacts Required:**
- Large protractor or circle division tool
- Ancient sundial replica or images
- Modern examples of circle math in use
- Grid paper for circle division practice

## B2: Structural Strength and Circular Design
**Duration:** 8-10 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will test and understand why circular structures are stronger than other shapes.

### Why Circles Are Strong
**Strength Testing Experiment:**
Create tubes from paper in different shapes:
- Circular tubes
- Square tubes  
- Triangular tubes

Test which can hold the most weight (books) before collapsing.

**Scientific Explanation (Grade 4 Level):**
- **Circular structures:** Force spreads evenly all around the circle
- **Angular structures:** Force gets "stuck" at corners, making weak spots
- **Even spreading:** Makes the whole structure stronger

### Engineering Applications
**Strong Circular Structures:**
- **Columns:** Round columns in buildings are stronger than square ones
- **Cans:** Food cans are circular because they won't dent as easily
- **Tunnels:** Round tunnels are safer because they don't have weak corners
- **Pipes:** Water flows better through circular pipes

### Hands-On Investigation
**Build and Test Activity:**

2. Fill with sand or rice to test capacity
3. Apply gentle pressure to test strength
4. Record which shapes fail first and how

**Real-World Connection:**
"Why do you think soda cans are circular instead of square? What would happen to a square can?"

## B6: Mathematical Problem-Solving Workshop
**Duration:** 10-12 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will solve multi-step problems involving circular measurements and applications.

### Grade 4 Circle Problems
**Problem Set 1: Measurement and Calculation**

**Problem A: Garden Planning**
"Sara wants to plant a circular flower garden. If she walks 12 steps across the garden (diameter), how many steps will she walk around the entire edge?"
- Solution: Distance around ≈ 3 × distance across = 3 × 12 = 36 steps

**Problem B: Pizza Party**
"A circular pizza has a radius of 8 inches. If we cut it into 8 equal slices, what angle will each slice be?"
- Solution: 360° ÷ 8 slices = 45° per slice

**Problem C: Wheel Distance**
"A bicycle wheel is 3 feet across. How far does the bike travel when the wheel turns 10 complete times?"
- Solution: Distance per turn ≈ 3 × 3 = 9 feet; 10 turns = 90 feet

### Problem Set 2: Application and Design
**Challenge Problems:**

**Design Problem:** "Create a circular playground that is 20 feet across. Plan where to place 16 children so they are equally spaced around the edge. How far apart should each child stand?"
- Solution: Distance around ≈ 20 × 3 = 60 feet; 60 ÷ 16 = 3.75 feet apart

**Efficiency Problem:** "Compare two storage containers: one circular (10 feet across) and one square (10 feet on each side). Which can hold more? Which uses less material for the walls?"
- Requires area and perimeter calculations

### Problem-Solving Strategies
**Steps to Success:**
1. **Identify what you know:** What measurements are given?
2. **Identify what you need to find:** Distance around, across, area, angles?
3. **Choose the right tool:** Circle patterns, division, multiplication
4. **Check your answer:** Does it make sense in real life?
5. **Explain your thinking:** How did you solve it?

### Real-World Application
**Where These Skills Matter:**
- Planning circular gardens or playgrounds
- Calculating materials needed for round projects
- Understanding how wheels and rotating objects work
- Solving fair-sharing problems with circular arrangements

**Artifacts Required:**
- Calculators for complex calculations
