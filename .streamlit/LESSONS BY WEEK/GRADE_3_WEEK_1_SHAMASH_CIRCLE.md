### Shamash's Amazing Journey
Tell Shamash's story like a fairy tale:

Shamash was the wisest judge of all. Because he traveled high in the sky in a perfect circle, he could see everything that happened on Earth. **Shamash's Special Powers:**
- **Sun Magic:** He could bring light and warmth to help plants grow
- **Fair Seeing:** His circular journey let him watch over everyone the same way  
- **Kind Heart:** He wanted everyone to be treated fairly and kindly

### The Circular Symbol
"Shamash's special symbol was a circle with lines coming out like sun rays. This wasn't just a pretty picture - it showed everyone something very important about fairness!"

**Why Circles Mean Fairness:**
- **Same Distance:** If you put people around a circle, everyone stands the same distance from the middle
- **No Best Spot:** Circles don't have corners, so there's no "best" or "worst" place to sit
- **Sharing Magic:** You can divide a circle into equal pieces, perfect for fair sharing
- **Never Ending:** Circles go round and round forever, just like Shamash's promise to be fair forever

## A4: Geometric Analysis - Discovering Circle Magic
**Duration:** 8-10 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will explore what makes circles special through hands-on investigation.

### Circle Detective Work
Using simple objects like plates, coins, and cups, students will be circle detectives!

**Circle Parts to Find:**
- **Center:** The very middle point (like the belly button of the circle!)
- **Edge:** The line that goes all the way around
- **Radius:** The distance from center to edge (always the same!)

### Hands-On Discoveries
**Activity 1: Paper Circle Folding**
1. Give each student a paper circle
2. Fold it in half, then in half again
3. Where the folds cross is the center!
4. Unfold and see the magic lines

**Activity 2: String Detective**
1. Use string to measure from center to edge at different spots
2. Cut strings and compare - they're all the same length!
3. Try the same thing with a square - what happens?

**Activity 3: Sharing Challenge**
"How would you fairly share a circular cookie with 4 friends? How about a square cookie?"
- Use paper circles and squares to practice fair division
- Count to see if pieces are really equal

### Amazing Discoveries
**What Our Circle Detectives Find:**
- The distance from center to edge is always the same (radius)
- Circles are perfect for fair sharing
- Circles don't have corners that make "bad" spots
- You can always find the exact center of a circle

**Decomposition Activity:** Break down what makes a circle:
- A center point (the most important spot)
- All points on the edge are equal distance from center
- No straight lines or corners

**Artifacts Required:**
- Paper circles and squares for cutting
- String, scissors, rulers
- Various circular objects for investigation
- Markers for marking centers and measuring

## A6: Mathematical History - Circles and Numbers
**Duration:** 6-7 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will discover how ancient people used circles to count and measure.

### Circle Counting Adventures
Ancient Mesopotamian people made cool discoveries by studying circles that we still use today!

**Amazing Circle Math:**
1. **Pizza Slice Math:** They figured out how to divide circles into equal parts - like cutting a pizza into fair slices!
2. **Time Circles:** They invented the first circular clocks to tell time using the sun's shadow
3. **Distance Around:** They discovered that if you walk around a circle, you go about 3 times as far as walking straight across it

### Ancient Word Problems (Kid-Friendly)
Let students solve these like ancient Mesopotamians:
1. "A baker wants to cut his round bread into 6 equal pieces for his family. How should he cut it?"
2. "A farmer wants to build a round pen for his sheep. If the pen is 4 steps across, about how many steps will he walk around the whole thing?"

**Modern Connections:**
- Pizza places still cut circular pizzas into equal slices
- Clocks are still circular to help us tell time
- Builders still use circle math to make round buildings

### Pattern Fun
**What Students Discover:**
- Cutting circles equally makes fair sharing easy
- The same circle math works for tiny coins and huge buildings
- Numbers and circles work together to solve problems

**Hands-On Activity:** Use paper plates as "ancient clocks" - mark numbers around the edge and use shadows to tell time like ancient people did!

### Circle Size Investigation
**Measurement Fun:**
Using different sized circular objects (plates, lids, coins), students will:

- Measure around (circumference) using string, then measure the string
- Record results on a simple chart

**Pattern Detective Work:**
"Look at your measurements. What do you notice?"

- Bigger circles go farther around

## B2: Building Strong Things with Circles
**Duration:** 8-9 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will test and discover why circular structures are stronger than other shapes.

### Strength Testing Fun
**Build and Test Experiment:**
Make tubes from paper in different shapes:
- Round tubes (like paper towel tubes)
- Square tubes
- Triangle tubes

**Kid-Friendly Science Explanation:**
- **Round tubes:** The pushing spreads out evenly all around the circle

### Strong Circles Everywhere

- **Cans:** Food cans are round so they don't dent easily

4. Record which shapes work best

**Real-World Question:** "Why do you think soda cans are round instead of square? What would happen to a square can if you dropped it?"

## B6: Math Problem-Solving Fun
**Duration:** 10-11 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will solve age-appropriate problems involving circles using basic math skills.

### Grade 3 Circle Problem Adventures
**Problem Set 1: Measurement and Sharing**

**Story Problem A: Pizza Party Planning**
"Emma's circular pizza is 6 paper clips across. About how many paper clips will it be around the outside?"
- Let students measure and discover: around is about 3 times across
- So 6 × 3 = about 18 paper clips around!

**Story Problem B: Fair Sharing**
"A round birthday cake needs to be cut into 8 equal pieces for 8 friends. What shape will each piece be?"
- Use paper circles to cut and discover: each piece is like a triangle with one curved side!

**Story Problem C: Circle Gardens**
"Marcus wants to plant flowers in a circle. If he plants one flower every step, and the circle is 12 steps around, how many flowers will he plant?"
- Walk it out: 12 steps = 12 flowers!

### Problem Set 2: Real-Life Applications
**Challenge Adventures:**

**Design Problem:** "Make a circular playground that's 8 steps across. If 16 children hold hands around the edge, how far apart should they stand?"
- Solution: 8 × 3 = 24 steps around; 24 ÷ 16 = about 1.5 steps apart

**Efficiency Problem:** "Which can hold more blocks: a round container or a square container that use the same amount of material for the walls?"
- Test with actual containers!

### Problem-Solving Helper Steps
**How to Solve Circle Problems:**
1. **Read carefully:** What do I know? What do I need to find?
2. **Draw it:** Make a picture of the circle problem
3. **Use what I learned:** Remember that around is about 3 times across
4. **Try it out:** Test with real objects when possible
5. **Check:** Does my answer make sense?

### Real-Life Math Applications
**Where These Skills Help:**
- Planning circular gardens or sandbox designs
- Figuring out how much material you need for round projects
- Understanding how wheels and spinning things work
- Solving fair-sharing problems with friends

**Artifacts Required:**
