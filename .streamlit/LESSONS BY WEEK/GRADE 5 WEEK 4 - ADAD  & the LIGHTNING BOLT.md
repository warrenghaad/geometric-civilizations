## DAY A: METAPHOR

**A2: SEL + Metaphor (Connection)**

Natural systems contain both gradual processes and sudden discontinuities. Rivers erode banks incrementally over centuries; earthquakes release accumulated stress in seconds. Pressure builds slowly in magma chambers; volcanic eruptions release that pressure catastrophically. Gradual accumulation followed by sudden release characterizes many physical phenomena.

The lightning bolt exemplifies this pattern in its purest form. Electrical charge separates gradually within storm clouds as updrafts carry water droplets and ice particles through regions of differing temperature. This separation builds potential difference—voltage—between cloud regions or between cloud and ground. When voltage exceeds the dielectric breakdown threshold of air (approximately 3 × 10⁶ V/m), current flows suddenly: the lightning stroke.

The stroke itself lasts microseconds to milliseconds. The buildup that preceded it lasted minutes to hours. The ratio of accumulation time to release time can exceed 10⁶. This extreme asymmetry—slow buildup, sudden release—defines the discontinuous event.

Adad, the Mesopotamian storm god, governed weather phenomena including rain, thunder, and lightning. His domain encompassed both agricultural necessity (rain for crops) and destructive power (floods, lightning strikes). The same deity controlled life-giving water and death-dealing storms—dual aspects unified in single divine figure, paralleling Ishtar's integration of love and war.

The lightning bolt symbol captures discontinuity geometrically: line segments meeting at acute angles, creating sharp directional changes. Unlike the smooth curves of the crescent or the radial symmetry of the star, the lightning bolt is angular, jagged, abrupt—visual representation of sudden change.

**A3: Symbolic Chain (Iconographic Art)**

LIGHTNING BOLT → ACUTE ANGLES → SUDDEN DIRECTIONAL CHANGE → ADAD → DISCONTINUOUS POWER

The lightning bolt consists of connected line segments meeting at acute angles. An acute angle measures less than 90°; the vertices of the lightning bolt symbol typically range from 20° to 60°.

**Formal angle classification:**

- Acute angle: 0° < θ < 90°
- Right angle: θ = 90°
- Obtuse angle: 90° < θ < 180°
- Straight angle: θ = 180°
- Reflex angle: 180° < θ < 360°

The lightning bolt's acute angles create visual "sharpness"—the perception of abrupt change. Psychophysical research confirms that humans perceive acute angles as more dynamic, aggressive, and energetic than obtuse angles. The lightning bolt symbol exploits this perceptual association.

**Actual lightning geometry:**

Real lightning follows a stepped leader path determined by local variations in air conductivity. The path is not straight because air is not homogeneous; the leader advances in discrete steps (typically 50 meters), changing direction at each step based on local conditions. The resulting path is a series of connected segments with angles determined by stochastic processes.

The idealized lightning bolt symbol abstracts this irregular path into a few representative angular changes, capturing the essential quality (abrupt directional changes) while simplifying the complexity.

**Adad's attributes:**

Adad (Akkadian; Sumerian equivalent: Iškur) controlled atmospheric phenomena. His symbols included:

- Lightning bolt (forked or zigzag line)
- Bull (associated with thunder—bull's roar resembling thunder)
- Lion-headed mace (weapon combining animal ferocity with striking function)

His sacred number was 6 or 10 depending on tradition and period. Temples to Adad existed throughout Mesopotamia, with major cult centers at Assur and Babylon.

**A5: Material Culture (Process)**

Adad's cult integrated with state religion and practical concerns about weather prediction across Mesopotamian history.

**Anu-Adad Temple at Assur:**

Assur (modern Qal'at Sherqat, Iraq) served as the original capital and religious center of Assyria. The Anu-Adad temple, a double temple dedicated to the sky god Anu and the storm god Adad, demonstrates the integration of these related deities.

The temple featured twin ziggurats—stepped towers for each deity. This architectural doubling is unusual; most temple complexes centered on a single ziggurat. The pairing reflects the theological relationship between Anu (sky, cosmic authority) and Adad (atmospheric phenomena within the sky).

Construction phases span from the Middle Assyrian period (circa 1300 BCE) through Neo-Assyrian renovations. German excavations (1903-1914) under Walter Andrae documented the temple complex.

**Ritual practices:**

Storm rituals sought to influence weather outcomes—requesting rain during drought, protection from destructive storms. These rituals combined offerings, incantations, and symbolic actions.

The "bull of heaven" mythology (appearing in the Epic of Gilgamesh) connects celestial bovine imagery to destructive storms. When Ishtar sends the Bull of Heaven against Gilgamesh, it causes drought and destruction—atmospheric catastrophe in mythological form.

**Agricultural integration:**

Adad's importance derived from agricultural dependence on rainfall. Southern Mesopotamia (Sumer, Babylon) relied primarily on irrigation from the Tigris and Euphrates, but northern Mesopotamia (Assyria) depended more heavily on direct rainfall. Adad's cult was correspondingly stronger in the north.

The timing of rains determined planting schedules, crop yields, and economic prosperity. Adad's favor—or disfavor—translated directly to material welfare. This practical stake elevated weather prediction (omen reading) to state concern.

**A6: Art Technique (Process)**

Constructing acute angles requires precise angular measurement or geometric construction methods.

**Acute angle construction (compass and straightedge):**

To construct a specific acute angle θ using compass and straightedge, one approach uses the relationship between angles and chord lengths in a unit circle.

For angle θ at vertex O:

1. Draw ray OA (one side of angle)
2. Draw circle of radius r centered at O
3. The chord length c subtending angle θ is: c = 2r × sin(θ/2)
4. Mark point B on the circle such that |AB| = c
5. Ray OB forms angle θ with ray OA

For common acute angles:

- 60°: c = 2r × sin(30°) = 2r × 0.5 = r (chord equals radius)
- 45°: c = 2r × sin(22.5°) ≈ 2r × 0.383 = 0.766r
- 30°: c = 2r × sin(15°) ≈ 2r × 0.259 = 0.518r

The 60° angle is especially easy: set compass to radius r, place point on circle, mark intersection—this point is exactly 60° from starting point.

**Angle bisection for arbitrary acute angles:**

Given an existing angle, bisection produces a smaller angle:

1. From vertex O, draw arc intersecting both sides at points A and B
2. From A and B, draw arcs of equal radius intersecting at point P
3. Line OP bisects angle AOB

Repeated bisection can produce arbitrarily small acute angles:

- Start with 90° (right angle, easily constructed)
- Bisect to get 45°
- Bisect to get 22.5°
- Bisect to get 11.25°
- Continue as needed

**Lightning bolt construction:**

1. Draw initial ray (first segment of bolt)
2. At endpoint, construct acute angle (30°-60° range) opening to one side
3. Draw second segment along new direction
4. At new endpoint, construct acute angle opening to opposite side (creating zigzag)
5. Continue alternating sides for additional segments
6. Maintain similar angle magnitudes for visual consistency

**B2: Math Proof (Proof)**

The wedge functions as a moving inclined plane, redirecting input force to produce output force perpendicular to the input direction. The acute angle determines the mechanical advantage.

**Inclined plane analysis:**

Consider a wedge with half-angle θ (the angle between one face and the central axis). The full wedge angle is 2θ.

When force F_input is applied along the wedge's axis (pushing it forward), the wedge faces exert force F_output perpendicular to themselves, pushing material apart.

Force balance at the wedge face (ignoring friction):

The input force component perpendicular to the wedge face must equal the reaction force from the material:

F_input × cos(θ) = N (normal force from material on wedge face)

The component of N that acts to separate the material (perpendicular to wedge axis) is:

F_output = N × cos(θ) = F_input × cos²(θ) / cos(θ) = F_input × cos(θ)

Wait—this analysis needs refinement. Let me reconsider using virtual work:

**Virtual work analysis:**

When the wedge advances distance d along its axis, each face moves perpendicular to itself by:

Δ = d × sin(θ)

By conservation of energy (ideal case, no friction):

F_input × d = 2 × F_output × Δ = 2 × F_output × d × sin(θ)

Solving for mechanical advantage:

MA = F_output / F_input = 1 / (2 × sin(θ))

**Mechanical advantage values:**

|Half-angle θ|Full angle 2θ|sin(θ)|MA = 1/(2sinθ)|
|---|---|---|---|
|5°|10°|0.087|5.74|
|10°|20°|0.174|2.87|
|15°|30°|0.259|1.93|
|20°|40°|0.342|1.46|
|30°|60°|0.500|1.00|

**Interpretation:**

Sharper wedges (smaller θ) provide greater mechanical advantage—more output force for given input force. However, sharper wedges require greater input distance to achieve given separation, and they are more susceptible to binding from friction.

The 10° half-angle (20° full angle) provides MA ≈ 2.87, meaning the splitting force is nearly three times the input force. This explains why thin wedges can split heavy logs with relatively modest hammer blows.

**Friction effects:**

Real wedges experience friction coefficient μ between faces and material. The effective mechanical advantage becomes:

MA_effective = 1 / (2 × (sin(θ) + μ × cos(θ)))

Friction reduces mechanical advantage and can cause wedges to bind (self-lock) when:

μ > tan(θ)

For μ = 0.3 (typical wood-on-wood), binding occurs when θ < arctan(0.3) ≈ 16.7°. Wedges sharper than this may require sustained force to prevent backing out.

**B3: Transformation (Operation)**

The wedge transforms the direction and magnitude of applied force through geometric relationships.

**Direction transformation:**

Input force: Axial (along wedge axis, typically horizontal for a splitting wedge) Output force: Lateral (perpendicular to wedge axis, pushing material apart)

The wedge rotates the force direction by 90°. This directional transformation is the wedge's primary function—converting available force direction (e.g., downward hammer blow or horizontal push) into needed force direction (lateral splitting).

**Magnitude transformation:**

The wedge amplifies force magnitude according to MA = 1/(2sinθ). A 1000 N input force on a wedge with θ = 10° produces 2870 N of lateral splitting force (ideal case).

This amplification comes at a cost: the input must travel farther than the output. If the wedge advances 10 cm, the material separates by only 10 × sin(10°) ≈ 1.74 cm per side (3.48 cm total separation).

**Energy conservation:**

Work_input = Work_output (ideal case)

F_input × d_input = F_output × d_output

Since F_output > F_input, it must be that d_output < d_input. The wedge trades distance for force, conserving total energy (work).

**Progressive penetration:**

As the wedge advances, wider portions engage the material. The separation increases linearly with penetration depth:

Total separation S at depth d: S = 2 × d × tan(θ)

For θ = 15° and d = 10 cm: S = 2 × 10 × tan(15°) = 2 × 10 × 0.268 = 5.36 cm

This progressive action allows the wedge to open cracks incrementally, with the leading edge initiating separation and subsequent portions widening it.

**B4: Mechanics (Result)**

The wedge represents one of the six classical simple machines. Its mechanical principles appear across diverse applications.

**Simple machine classification:**

The six classical simple machines (identified in antiquity and formalized during the Renaissance) are:

1. Lever
2. Wheel and axle
3. Pulley
4. Inclined plane
5. Wedge
6. Screw

The wedge is a moving inclined plane—the plane moves through material rather than material moving up the plane. The screw is a wedge wrapped helically around a cylinder. These three (inclined plane, wedge, screw) form a related family based on the same geometric principle.

**Cutting tool analysis:**

Knives, axes, and chisels function as wedges. The blade cross-section shows the characteristic acute angle.

Knife blade geometry:

- Edge angle (full angle): typically 15°-30° for kitchen knives, 25°-40° for utility knives
- Smaller angles = sharper edge, easier cutting, but more fragile
- Larger angles = more durable edge, but requires more force

The trade-off between sharpness (mechanical advantage) and durability (structural integrity) determines optimal blade geometry for each application.

**Wood splitting:**

A splitting wedge (maul or wedge insert) typically has a 20°-30° angle (10°-15° half-angle). The wedge is driven into the end grain of a log. The lateral force separates wood fibers along the grain, splitting the log.

Wood splits more easily along the grain than across it because the grain represents fiber alignment. The wedge exploits this anisotropy—applying force perpendicular to fibers where resistance is lowest.

**Doorstop mechanics:**

A doorstop wedge uses friction and geometry to hold a door open. When the door presses against the wedge, the wedge's angle converts the horizontal door force into vertical friction force against the floor:

F_friction = μ × F_normal = μ × F_door × cot(θ)

For a shallow wedge (small θ), cot(θ) is large, creating high friction force that resists door movement. The doorstop converts the door's own force into the force that holds it in place—a clever use of wedge geometry.

**B5: STEM History (Context)**

Wedge technology spans human history from Paleolithic hand axes to modern engineering applications.

**Paleolithic hand axes (circa 1.7 million years ago - 100,000 years ago):**

Acheulean hand axes represent some of the earliest standardized tools. These bifacially flaked stone implements feature a characteristic teardrop shape with an acute edge around much of the perimeter. The working edges typically form angles of 25°-55° depending on intended function (cutting vs. chopping).

The consistency of hand axe form across vast geographic areas (Africa, Europe, Asia) and time spans (over 1 million years) suggests either cultural transmission or independent discovery of optimal wedge geometry.

Homo erectus and early Homo sapiens produced these tools. The cognitive requirements—envisioning the finished form within the raw stone, executing a sequence of precise strikes—represent significant mental capabilities.

**Mesopotamian applications:**

Bronze Age Mesopotamia used wedge principles in:

- Axes and adzes for woodworking
- Chisels for stone and wood carving
- Plows (ard) for agricultural tillage
- Cuneiform stylus (wedge-shaped tip for clay impression)

The cuneiform writing system itself—named from Latin "cuneus" (wedge)—employed wedge-shaped impressions. The stylus tip formed a small wedge that, pressed into clay at various angles, created the distinctive marks of the script.

**Quarrying technology:**

Ancient quarrying used the "plug and feather" method: a line of holes was drilled into rock, then wedge-shaped plugs inserted between feathers (metal shims). Driving the plugs inward created lateral force along the hole line, splitting the rock predictably.

Egyptian obelisks, some weighing over 300 tons, were quarried using this technique. The controlled application of wedge force allowed extraction of massive monolithic stones.

**Modern engineering:**

Wedge principles appear in:

- Machine tool bits (lathe cutting tools, drill bits)
- Hydraulic splitters (applying wedge geometry with hydraulic force)
- Nail and screw tips
- Zipper mechanisms (interlocking wedges)

The fundamental mechanics remain unchanged from Paleolithic hand axes to modern precision cutting tools. Only materials, power sources, and precision have advanced.

**B6: Invention Moment (Specific Breakthrough)**

**Core insight:** Acute angles at a tool's working edge concentrate and redirect applied force, enabling cutting and splitting with less effort than blunt edges.

This insight represents one of humanity's earliest technological discoveries—predating written language, agriculture, and metallurgy by over a million years.

**Discovery pathway:**

Natural sharp edges—broken stones, shells, bone fragments—provided initial models. Early hominins likely used fortuitously sharp natural objects before deliberately creating them.

The transition to deliberate tool-making required recognizing that:

1. Sharp edges are useful (observation)
2. Sharp edges can be created by removing material (insight)
3. Systematic removal produces consistent results (technique)

Lithic reduction (stone tool making) became increasingly sophisticated:

- Oldowan tools (circa 2.6 million years ago): Simple flakes with sharp edges, minimal shaping
- Acheulean tools (circa 1.7 million years ago): Bifacial shaping producing symmetric wedge forms
- Mousterian tools (circa 300,000 years ago): Prepared core techniques for controlled flake production
- Later industries: Blade technologies, pressure flaking, hafting

Each development refined control over edge angle, with craftspeople implicitly understanding (through practice, not theory) that specific angles suited specific tasks.

**Quantifying prehistoric achievement:**

An Acheulean hand axe with a 30° edge angle (15° half-angle) provides MA ≈ 1.93. The tool nearly doubles the force applied.

A finely made Solutrean blade (circa 22,000 years ago) might achieve edge angles of 20° or less, providing MA > 2.5.

These prehistoric toolmakers optimized wedge geometry through millennia of empirical refinement—arriving at solutions that modern engineering analysis confirms as mechanically optimal.

**B7: Engineering Activity (Apply)**

**Materials:**

- Stiff cardboard or foam board for wedges
- Protractor
- Ruler
- Modeling clay or dense foam block (consistent material)
- Small weights or books (for standardized loading)
- Vernier calipers or precision ruler
- Data recording sheet

**Wedge construction:**

Construct three wedges from identical cardboard:

- Wedge A: 20° full angle (10° half-angle)
- Wedge B: 40° full angle (20° half-angle)
- Wedge C: 60° full angle (30° half-angle)

All wedges should have identical height (e.g., 8 cm) and width (e.g., 3 cm). Only the angle differs.

**Testing protocol:**

1. Prepare fresh, flat surface on clay block
2. Position Wedge A vertically, point down, on clay surface
3. Place standardized weight (e.g., 500g) on top of wedge
4. Wait 10 seconds for full penetration
5. Measure penetration depth (d) using calipers
6. Carefully remove wedge without disturbing clay
7. Measure separation width (S) at clay surface
8. Record both measurements
9. Repeat with fresh clay surface (minimum 3 trials per wedge)
10. Repeat entire procedure for Wedges B and C

**Data analysis:**

For each wedge:

- Calculate mean penetration depth (d̄)
- Calculate mean separation width (S̄)
- Calculate ratio S̄/d̄ (should approximate 2×tan(θ))
- Compare to theoretical: S/d = 2×tan(θ)
