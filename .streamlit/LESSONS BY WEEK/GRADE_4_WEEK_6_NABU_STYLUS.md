## A3: Deity Introduction - Nabu, The Knowledge Keeper
**Duration:** 6-7 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will understand how Nabu served as divine scribe and librarian, connecting writing tools to knowledge preservation and sharing.

### Nabu's Important Work
Nabu had one of the most crucial jobs among ancient gods: preserving all important knowledge for future generations. As the god of writing and wisdom, he made sure that laws, stories, scientific discoveries, and cultural knowledge would never be forgotten.

**Nabu's Knowledge Powers:**
- **Recording Laws:** Wrote down rules and regulations so everyone knew what was expected
- **Preserving Stories:** Saved important myths, histories, and cultural traditions
- **Tracking Information:** Kept records of trade, treaties, and important events
- **Teaching Communication:** Helped humans develop better ways to share ideas

### The Stylus Symbol
Nabu's symbol was the stylus because it represented the power to make ideas permanent and shareable. The stylus's wedge-shaped tip could create clear, lasting marks in clay:

**Why Styluses Represent Knowledge and Communication:**
- **Precision:** Sharp point allows detailed, accurate writing
- **Permanence:** Creates marks that last for thousands of years
- **Clarity:** Wedge shape makes distinct, readable symbols
- **Efficiency:** Simple tool that anyone can learn to use effectively

**Interactive Question:** "If you could only save three important ideas for future students to learn, what would they be and why?"

**Artifacts Required:**
- Large stylus model demonstrating wedge tip and writing technique
- Ancient cuneiform tablets showing Nabu's influence
- Examples of important knowledge preserved through writing

## A4: Geometric Analysis - Stylus Design Properties
**Duration:** 8-10 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will discover how stylus geometry creates effective writing tools and analyze angles and shapes that improve communication.

### Exploring Stylus Geometry
Using clay, different stylus shapes, and measurement tools, students investigate:

**Stylus Design Elements:**
- **Tip Angle:** How sharp or blunt the writing end is
- **Wedge Shape:** The triangular cross-section that creates clear marks
- **Length:** How long the stylus is for comfortable writing
- **Thickness:** How wide the tool is for good grip and control

### Hands-On Investigation
**Writing Tool Testing:**
1. **Angle Testing:** Try writing with styluses at different tip angles - which creates clearest marks?
2. **Shape Comparison:** Test round vs. wedge vs. flat tips on clay
3. **Size Analysis:** Compare comfort and control with different stylus lengths
4. **Pressure Testing:** Discover how much pressure creates optimal marks without damaging clay

**Mathematical Discoveries:**
- Specific angles work best for clear, consistent writing
- Wedge shapes create more distinct symbols than round shapes
- Length affects writing comfort and precision
- Optimal pressure creates lasting marks without breaking tools

**Decomposition Activity:** Break down stylus design into functional components:
- Tip geometry (creates the actual marks)
- Shaft design (provides grip and control)
- Material properties (durability and comfort)
- Size proportions (balance of precision and usability)

**Artifacts Required:**
- Clay tablets and various stylus designs for testing
- Measuring tools for analyzing tip angles and dimensions
- Different materials for creating stylus prototypes

## A5: Material Culture - Writing Systems in Mesopotamian Society
**Duration:** 8-10 minutes  
**Learning Objective:** Students will identify how stylus-based writing systems enabled record-keeping, education, and cultural development in ancient Mesopotamia.

### Ancient Communication Revolution
Mesopotamians developed the world's first writing system using stylus tools, which revolutionized how their civilization organized information, education, and cultural preservation.

**Record-Keeping Systems:**
- **Government Records:** Laws, treaties, tax records, and official communications
- **Trade Documentation:** Contracts, receipts, inventory lists, and shipping records
