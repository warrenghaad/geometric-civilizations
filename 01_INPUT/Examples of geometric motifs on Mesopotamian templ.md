# Examples of geometric motifs on Mesopotamian temple façades

Mesopotamian temple façades use a small, fairly repeatable toolkit of geometric motifs, applied in different media and scales.[^1][^2]

## Cone mosaics (Uruk, early temples)

- Sumerian builders pressed thousands of small, baked clay cones (about 5–10 cm long) into wet plaster so that only the painted circular ends showed, usually in red, black, and white. Arranged in rows, these produced repeating **triangles, diamonds/lozenges, zigzags, and checkerboard fields** across temple walls, especially in the Eanna precinct at Uruk and other southern Mesopotamian sites.[^3][^4][^5][^6][^7][^8]
- Common façade motifs from cone mosaics include:
    - Horizontal bands of black or red **triangles** pointing up or down
    - Nested or alternating **diamond (lozenge) grids**
    - Large **zigzag chevrons** marching along the wall[^4][^7][^8]


## Niched-and-buttressed “palace façade”

- Many late Uruk and Early Dynastic temples use **vertical niches and buttresses** that break a flat wall into a rhythmic pattern of shallow projections and recesses.[^9][^2][^1]
- In plan and elevation this produces:
    - Repeating rectangular **panels** framed by pilaster‑like buttresses
    - “Compound” or nested niches, where larger recesses contain smaller ones, creating stepped, tiered rectilinear patterns along the whole façade[^10][^11][^1]


## Glazed-brick geometry on Neo‑Babylonian façades

- The Ishtar Gate and its associated processional walls use molded glazed bricks to create repeating **rosette** bands, **linear borders**, and measured rows of animals.[^12][^13][^14]
- Key motifs:
    - **Rosettes**: round, stylized flowers (often 8‑petaled) set at regular intervals in horizontal bands, acting as fertility and Ishtar symbols while forming a strict radial geometric unit repeated across the wall.[^15][^16][^12]
    - **Strip borders** framing panels and animals—straight bands, stepped edges, and double lines that divide the façade into geometric zones at fixed heights.[^13][^14][^12]
    - **Gridded animal placement**: lions, bulls, and mušḫuššu dragons are placed in even horizontal rows and vertical alignments, so that each figure occupies a measured “cell” in an invisible grid.[^17][^12][^13]


## Ziggurat façades: steps, panels, and bands

- Classical ziggurats use **stepped stages**—stacked rectangular platforms—which generate strong horizontal bands and vertical risers visible on all sides.[^2][^18][^19]
- Their brick sheathing often emphasizes:
    - Regular **course lines** (horizontal brick bands)
    - Repeating **paneling** or light buttressing that echoes the palace-façade motif on the tower’s base and upper stories[^8][^11][^2]


## Other recurring geometric surface motifs

- Sculpted and painted temple or palace walls can include small-scale **spirals, leaf-scrolls, cone and grape clusters, and simple chevron borders**, usually as fillers in friezes or around doorways, all arranged in tightly controlled, repeating units.[^17][^8]
- Across media (cone, brick, plaster), the key façade motifs are thus:
    - **Triangles and zigzags**
    - **Diamonds/lozenges and checkerboards**
    - **Rectangular niche/buttress paneling**
    - **Radial rosettes and linear framing bands**[^6][^4][^2][^8]
